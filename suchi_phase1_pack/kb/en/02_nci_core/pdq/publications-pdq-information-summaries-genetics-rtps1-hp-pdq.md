# Rhabdoid Tumor Predisposition Syndrome Type 1 (PDQ®) - NCI

# Rhabdoid Tumor Predisposition Syndrome Type 1 (PDQ®)–Health Professional Version

## Introduction

Rhabdoid tumor [predisposition syndrome](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=809371&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) type 1 (RTPS1) is caused by [germline](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=460154&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic), or hereditary, [heterozygous](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=339341&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) loss-of-function [pathogenic variants](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=783960&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) in the *SMARCB1* [gene](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45693&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) on [chromosome](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46470&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) 22q11.[[1](#cit/section_1.1)-[3](#cit/section_1.3)] Individuals with RTPS1 are at an increased risk of developing both central nervous system (CNS) and non-CNS rhabdoid tumors, including atypical teratoid/rhabdoid tumors (AT/RTs), malignant rhabdoid tumors (MRTs) of the kidney, and extrarenal rhabdoid tumors. These tumors tend to require multimodal treatment such as surgery, chemotherapy, and possibly radiation therapy. These tumors occur most frequently in early childhood, often before the age of 3 years.

## Nomenclature

Individuals who harbor a [germline](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=460154&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) [pathogenic variant](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=783960&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) in *SMARCB1* are said to have rhabdoid tumor [predisposition syndrome](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=809371&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) type 1 (RTPS1).[[1](#cit/section_2.1)] Individuals who harbor a germline pathogenic [variant](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=776887&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) in *SMARCA4* are said to have rhabdoid tumor predisposition syndrome type 2 (RTPS2).[[2](#cit/section_2.2)] Nomenclature in genetic disorders continues to evolve, and future terminology for these disorders may reflect the proposed dyadic naming approach, incorporating both [gene](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45693&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) and [phenotype](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=460203&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic).[[3](#cit/section_2.3)]

## Genetics

### Molecular Genetics

The *SMARCB1* [gene](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45693&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) encodes the SMARCB1 protein (also called INI1 or BAF47), a member of the SWI/SNF chromatin remodeling complex, which helps control gene [transcription](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=390290&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic).[[1](#cit/section_3.1),[2](#cit/section_3.2)] Rhabdoid tumors develop when both copies of the *SMARCB1* gene are inactivated, or in some cases, when both copies of the *SMARCA4* gene are inactivated. Individuals with [germline](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=460154&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) *SMARCA4* [variants](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=776887&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) have a diagnosis of rhabdoid tumor predisposition syndrome type 2. Loss of SMARCB1/INI1 expression (detected by immunohistochemistry) is included in the diagnostic criteria for rhabdoid tumors.[[3](#cit/section_3.3)]

In individuals with rhabdoid tumor [predisposition syndrome](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=809371&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) type 1 (RTPS1), tumor development follows the Knudsen two-hit hypothesis. The first hit is caused by the inactivation of one copy of *SMARCB1*, typically by a truncating variant, [copy-number change](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=600383&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic), or structural variant. The second hit is typically due to [chromosomal](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46470&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) [deletion](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=460141&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) or [loss of heterozygosity](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=464169&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic).[[4](#cit/section_3.4)] It is estimated that approximately 25% to 35% of rhabdoid tumor cases are associated with a [germline variant](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=781852&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) in *SMARCB1*.[[5](#cit/section_3.5)-[7](#cit/section_3.7)] Higher rates have been reported in certain subgroups, including patients diagnosed at younger than 6 months (55%) and patients with multifocal tumors (78%).[[8](#cit/section_3.8),[9](#cit/section_3.9)] It is recommended that all patients diagnosed with rhabdoid tumors consider genetic evaluation.[[10](#cit/section_3.10)] Whole- and partial-gene deletions, [nonsense](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=783965&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic), and [frameshift variants](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=783963&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) are seen most commonly, frequently affecting [exons](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=460146&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) 2 through 7 of the *SMARCB1* gene.[[5](#cit/section_3.5),[6](#cit/section_3.6),[11](#cit/section_3.11)] [Single nucleotide variants](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=803525&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic), duplications, and [splice-site variants](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=783968&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) occur more rarely.[[5](#cit/section_3.5),[11](#cit/section_3.11),[12](#cit/section_3.12)] A case of ring chromosome 22 has also been reported in an individual with RTPS1.[[13](#cit/section_3.13)]

### Inheritance

RTPS1 is inherited in an [autosomal dominant](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=793860&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) manner. [Penetrance](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=339344&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) is reported as greater than 90% for most types of truncating variants in *SMARCB1*.[[7](#cit/section_3.7)] However, these estimates are based on a modest number of observations and ascertainment of affected individuals and families. Most cases appear to be [de novo](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=460142&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic), and individuals with rhabdoid tumors are often the first person in the family to be affected.[[6](#cit/section_3.6),[14](#cit/section_3.14),[15](#cit/section_3.15)] This may be due to the aggressive nature of rhabdoid tumors. However, [incomplete penetrance](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=792352&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) and [late-onset](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=339332&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) tumors, including schwannoma and meningioma, have been reported in a small subset of families who are [unaffected](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=460224&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) by rhabdoid tumors and carry a germline *SMARCB1* variant.[[5](#cit/section_3.5),[11](#cit/section_3.11)] [Gonadal mosaicism](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=813317&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) in a parent has been well documented in RTPS1. Therefore, negative genetic testing of parental blood cannot rule out the risk that more than one child may inherit the pathogenic variant and have a diagnosis of RTPS1.[[5](#cit/section_3.5),[6](#cit/section_3.6),[11](#cit/section_3.11),[16](#cit/section_3.16),[17](#cit/section_3.17)] [Genetic counseling](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44961&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) has been suggested to discuss topics like prenatal testing and potential reproductive [recurrence risk](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=460213&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) relevant to gonadal mosaicism.[[5](#cit/section_3.5),[6](#cit/section_3.6)]

### Related Disorders

Germline alterations in the *SMARCB1* gene are primarily associated with two additional [hereditary conditions](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=809370&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic), schwannomatosis and Coffin-Siris syndrome (CSS). Cases of schwannoma have been reported in RTPS1 kindreds. However, rhabdoid tumors have not been reported in individuals with CSS. Generally, *SMARCB1* variants associated with CSS are [missense variants](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=783964&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) that cluster in the first and last exons of the gene.[[11](#cit/section_3.11)] In patients with schwannomatosis, variants are often nontruncating and occur in the 3’ untranslated (UTR) region of *SMARCB1*.[[18](#cit/section_3.18)] However, [nonsense](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=783965&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) *SMARCB1* variants or deletions/duplications of the whole gene/multiple exons are more often associated with rhabdoid tumors.[[11](#cit/section_3.11),[18](#cit/section_3.18)] While these patterns represent the common [genotype](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=660739&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic)-[phenotype](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=460203&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) correlations, they are not final, and overlap can be seen. However, exon-spanning deletions appear to occur exclusively in patients with RTPS1.[[11](#cit/section_3.11)] While genotype-phenotype correlations exist across these three disorders, there is limited information regarding genotype-phenotype correlations specific to RTPS1.

## Cancer Risks, Spectrum, and Characteristics

As a class, [copy-number](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=600383&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) [(deletion)](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=460141&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) and truncating variants in *SMARCB1* are associated with increased risk of tumor predisposition, although there are no known *SMARCB1* [genotypes](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=660739&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) that can predict whether a tumor (or type of tumor) will develop.[[1](#cit/section_4.1)] A meta-analysis of 185 carriers of *SMARCB1* [variants](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=776887&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) found that the most common tumor type was atypical teratoid/rhabdoid tumors (AT/RTs), followed by rhabdoid tumors of the kidney and soft tissue.[[1](#cit/section_4.1)] Multifocal tumors were common. Other tumors found included meningiomas, malignant peripheral nerve sheath tumors, chondrosarcomas, epithelioid sarcomas, leiomyosarcomas, leiomyomas, and myoepitheliomas. Anatomical locations of the tumors varied, especially in the brain.[[2](#cit/section_4.2)] Tumors can also be [synchronous](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=815374&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) or [metachronous](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=815372&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic).

In patients with rhabdoid tumor predisposition syndrome type 1 (RTPS1), tumor onset typically occurs in very early childhood (often in infancy), and tumors may even be detected pre- or perinatally. There are rare case reports of adults developing *SMARCB1*-associated tumors. As [next-generation sequencing](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=763024&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) of tumors has become more prevalent, alterations in the *SMARCB1* [gene](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45693&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) may require investigation for an underlying [germline](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=460154&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) predisposition. However, the absence of a genetic alteration in the *SMARBC1* gene does not rule out an underlying [cancer predisposition syndrome](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=754220&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic).[[3](#cit/section_4.3)] Second primary tumors have developed up to 15 years after the first primary tumor diagnosis, suggesting that lifetime [surveillance](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=496506&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) is needed for patients with this syndrome.[[4](#cit/section_4.4),[5](#cit/section_4.5)]

## Management

### Genetic Counseling

Experts in the field of cancer genetics recommend a referral for [genetic counseling](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44961&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) or genetic evaluation for children who have been diagnosed with atypical teratoid/rhabdoid tumors (AT/RTs), malignant rhaboid tumors (MRTs), or any other SMARCB1-deficient tumors. These recommendations are based on the American Association for Cancer Research's (AACR) Childhood Cancer Predisposition Workshop and the European Society for Paediatric Oncology's (SIOPE) Host Genome Working Group guidelines.[[1](#cit/section_5.1),[2](#cit/section_5.2)] In the event that a child with an AT/RT or MRT died before completing the evaluation, counseling may be provided to parents and siblings.

### Rhabdoid Tumor Predisposition and Fertility Preservation

Treatments for AT/RTs and MRTs may reduce fertility in boys and girls.[[3](#cit/section_5.3),[4](#cit/section_5.4)] Therefore, the medical team may offer fertility preservation to these patients. Parents of children with AT/RTs and MRTs who undergo fertility preservation should be advised that if a [germline](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=460154&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) *SMARCB1* truncating [variant](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=776887&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) is found, 50% of children derived from preserved gonadal tissue will inherit a *SMARCB1* pathogenic variant and have a risk of developing rhabdoid tumors. Prenatal genetic counseling is advised for individuals with RTPS1.

### Outcomes and Treatment Modification

In patients with AT/RTs or extracranial rhabdoid tumors, germline *SMARCB1* variants are associated with poor outcomes. The risk of metastasis may also be associated with a [germline variant](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=781852&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic).[[5](#cit/section_5.5),[6](#cit/section_5.6)] Despite intensive therapy, fewer than half of children with rhabdoid tumor predisposition syndrome type 1 (RTPS1) become long-term survivors. There are no data suggesting that treatment of patients with AT/RTs or MRTs should be modified because of a germline RTPS1 diagnosis.

For information about the treatment of AT/RTs, see [Childhood Central Nervous System Atypical Teratoid/Rhabdoid Tumor Treatment](/types/brain/hp/child-cns-atrt-treatment-pdq). For information about the treatment of MRTs, see the [Rhabdoid Tumors of the Kidney](/types/kidney/hp/wilms-treatment-pdq#_17) section in Wilms Tumor and Other Childhood Kidney Tumors Treatment and the [Rhabdoid Tumor NOS (Extrarenal)](/types/soft-tissue-sarcoma/hp/child-soft-tissue-treatment-pdq#_628) section in Childhood Soft Tissue Sarcoma Treatment.

### Surveillance

In 2017, a group of experts in childhood cancer genetics created the first recommendations for cancer [surveillance](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=496506&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) for patients with RTPS1.[[7](#cit/section_5.7)] Since children with germline *SMARCB1* truncating variants have an increased risk of developing AT/RTs in early childhood, [screening](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46171&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) recommendations include brain magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) every 3 months until age 5 years, after which surveillance ceases. Because of the risk of MRTs of the kidney, abdominal ultrasonography is recommended every 3 months from birth, with no upper age limit to stop surveillance. Whole-body MRI may also be considered until age 5 years. Screening is not recommended for children with a germline [missense](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=783964&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) [pathogenic variant](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=783960&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) in the *SMARCB1* [gene](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45693&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) because the risk of tumor development is very low.

In 2021, the SIOPE Host Genome Working Group published guidelines for clinical surveillance and genetic testing in patients with RTPS1.[[2](#cit/section_5.2)] These guidelines recommend an age-based approach to surveillance. Central nervous system (CNS) and abdominal imaging occur every 1 to 3 months for patients younger than 6 months, every 2 to 3 months for patients aged 7 to 18 months, and every 3 months for patients aged 19 months to 5 years. Yearly whole-body MRI is recommended for patients aged 5 years and older.

### Surveillance in Survivorship

For children with tumors and *SMARCB1* truncating variants, surveillance should be ongoing to detect primary disease recurrence. Survivors of AT/RTs will continue to require abdominal ultrasonography to screen for MRTs. Conversely, children with MRTs of the kidney will continue to undergo CNS surveillance for AT/RTs until age 5 years if they have a germline truncating variant in *SMARCB1*.[[2](#cit/section_5.2)]
