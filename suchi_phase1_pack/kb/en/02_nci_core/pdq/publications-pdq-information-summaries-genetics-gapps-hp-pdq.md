# Gastric Adenocarcinoma and Proximal Polyposis of the Stomach (GAPPS) (PDQ®) - NCI

# Gastric Adenocarcinoma and Proximal Polyposis of the Stomach (GAPPS) (PDQ®)–Health Professional Version

## Introduction to Gastric Adenocarcinoma and Proximal Polyposis of the Stomach (GAPPS)

Gastric adenocarcinoma and proximal polyposis of the stomach (GAPPS) is an [autosomal dominant](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=793860&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) syndrome caused by [germline](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=460154&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) [pathogenic variants](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=783960&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) in promoter 1B of the *APC* [gene](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45693&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic).[[1](#cit/section_1.1),[2](#cit/section_1.2)] Of note, large [deletions](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=460141&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) involving promoter 1B of the *APC* gene are associated with a different syndrome called familial adenomatous polyposis (FAP).[[3](#cit/section_1.3),[4](#cit/section_1.4)] GAPPS was first described in 2012.[[2](#cit/section_1.2)] This syndrome is characterized by numerous stomach polyps (predominantly fundic gland polyps in the body and fundus of the stomach, sparing the antrum), with regions of dysplasia.[[2](#cit/section_1.2),[5](#cit/section_1.5)] GAPPS is not typically associated with colorectal polyposis. The risk of intestinal-type gastric adenocarcinoma appears to be significantly elevated in GAPPS, but the exact risk of this is currently unknown.

GAPPS and FAP are both caused by pathogenic or likely pathogenic variants in the *APC* gene, but they are distinct syndromes with different clinical characteristics. In GAPPS, polyposis and cancer risk are primarily confined to the stomach, while in FAP, polyposis and cancer risk are elevated in the colon. FAP is a [hereditary cancer syndrome](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=754220&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) characterized by colorectal polyposis and a 93% risk of colorectal adenocarcinoma. Pathogenic or likely pathogenic variants in promoter 1B of the *APC* gene are [pathognomonic](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=454769&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) of GAPPS and do not lead to FAP. In contrast, FAP is caused by germline pathogenic variants elsewhere in the *APC* gene or large deletions in promoter 1B. For more information about FAP, refer to the [FAP](/types/colorectal/hp/colorectal-genetics-pdq#_3205) section in Genetics of Colorectal Cancer.

Environmental risk factors have not been reported to cause or modify the [penetrance](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=339344&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) of GAPPS. Although studies have speculated that there is an inverse relationship between gastric neoplasia and *Helicobacter pylori* infection, further research is warranted to clarify how *H. pylori* impacts gastric neoplasia in individuals with GAPPS.[[6](#cit/section_1.6)]

## Genetics of GAPPS

Gastric adenocarcinoma and proximal polyposis of the stomach (GAPPS) is caused by [germline](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=460154&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) [pathogenic variants](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=783960&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) in promoter 1B (regulatory region) of the *APC* [tumor suppressor gene](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46657&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic).[[1](#cit/section_2.1)] Of note, large [deletions](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=460141&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) involving promoter 1B result in a familial adenomatous polyposis (FAP) [phenotype](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=460203&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic). For more information, see the [FAP](/types/colorectal/hp/colorectal-genetics-pdq#_3205) section in Genetics of Colorectal Cancer.[[2](#cit/section_2.2),[3](#cit/section_2.3)]

The *APC* [gene](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45693&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) is located on [chromosome](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46470&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) 5q21 and encodes a 2,843–amino acid protein that is important for cell adhesion and signal transduction processes.[[4](#cit/section_2.4),[5](#cit/section_2.5)] The APC protein regulates intracellular concentrations of beta-catenin, a major mediator of the Wnt signal transduction pathway.[[6](#cit/section_2.6)] For more information about the *APC* gene, see the [Genetics of FAP](/types/colorectal/hp/colorectal-genetics-pdq#_3253) section in Genetics of Colorectal Cancer.

A 2016 study identified that germline pathogenic variants in promoter 1B of the *APC* gene cause GAPPS.[[1](#cit/section_2.1)] The study included six families who all previously tested negative for germline pathogenic variants in *APC*. However, regulatory regions in *APC*, like promoter 1B, were not included in their original testing.[[1](#cit/section_2.1),[7](#cit/section_2.7)] [Whole genome sequencing](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=740456&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) was also performed in these families, and it failed to identify novel or rare genetic variants that cosegregated with GAPPS. After a [loss of heterozygosity](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=464169&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) was identified in fundic gland polyps on chromosome 5 in a 46-Mb region containing the *APC* gene, the researchers pursued [Sanger sequencing](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=763028&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) of the *APC* gene's promoter 1A and promoter 1B in family members affected with GAPPS.[[1](#cit/section_2.1)] Sanger sequencing identified several pathogenic variants in the promoter 1B region of *APC*, including *APC* c.-191T>C variants in four families with GAPPS, an *APC* c.-192A>G variant in one family with GAPPS, and an *APC* c.-195A>C variant in one family with GAPPS. These *APC* pathogenic variants reside within the Ying Yang 1 binding motif of promoter 1B, and hence, reduce [transcriptional](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=390290&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) activity of *APC*. The study also reported that a second [somatic](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=781854&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) hit in *APC* occurred in most fundic gland polyps in relatives with GAPPS, either via loss of the wild-type [allele](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=339337&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) or acquisition of protein-truncating variants.

Since GAPPS was first described in the literature, over 20 families with the syndrome have been identified in Australia, North America, Europe, and Japan.[[1](#cit/section_2.1),[7](#cit/section_2.7)-[12](#cit/section_2.12)] However, the population prevalence and geographic distribution of GAPPS is elusive. This phenomenon may be due to the following: 1) disparate access to diagnostic [screening](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46171&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) modalities that can identify gastric lesions, and 2) few laboratories offer sequencing analysis of *APC*'s promoter 1B.[[13](#cit/section_2.13),[14](#cit/section_2.14)]

## Genetic Testing for GAPPS

Consensus genetic testing guidelines specific to gastric adenocarcinoma and proximal polyposis of the stomach (GAPPS) do not yet exist. Proposed diagnostic criteria for GAPPS include a [heterozygous](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=339341&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) [germline](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=460154&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) *APC* [pathogenic variant](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=783960&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) in promoter 1B **and** all of the following:[[1](#cit/section_3.1),[2](#cit/section_3.2)]

- More than 100 proximal gastric polyps with antral sparing in the [proband](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=460211&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) **or** more than 30 gastric polyps in an individual who has a [first-degree relative](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=460150&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) with GAPPS.
- The proband's gastric polyps primarily have fundic gland pathology (some with dysplasia), **or** the proband has a family member with a history of dysplastic fundic gland polyps or gastric adenocarcinoma.
- Proband does not have a history of duodenal or colorectal polyposis.
- Gastric polyps and gastric adenocarcinoma in the family follow an [autosomal dominant](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=793860&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) inheritance pattern.
- Other causes of gastric polyposis have been ruled out (i.e., other [heritable](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=781848&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) gastric polyposis syndromes and the use of proton pump inhibitors).

However, GAPPS should be suspected in patients with 100 or more proximal gastric polyps with antral sparing, the absence of colorectal polyposis, and the presence of a germline pathogenic [variant](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=776887&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) in the promoter 1B region of the *APC* [gene](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45693&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic). Individuals are at the greatest risk for GAPPS if they have a biological relative with clinical features of GAPPS and/or a known germline pathogenic variant in promoter 1B of the *APC* gene. Clinical-grade, diagnostic genetic testing for GAPPS is available at Clinical Laboratory Improvement Amendments (CLIA)-certified laboratories. Although many laboratories in the United States offer germline *APC* genetic testing, only a few perform sequence analysis of promoter 1B for the pathogenic variants that cause GAPPS.[[3](#cit/section_3.3)] Therefore, when patients have personal and/or [family histories](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=302456&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) that are suggestive of GAPPS, *APC* genetic testing must be ordered from one of these laboratories.

Primary differential diagnoses for GAPPS include the following:

- **Classic and attenuated familial adenomatous polyposis (FAP)**. In both classic and attenuated FAP, fundic gland polyps are observed in up to 89% of cases (fundic gland polyps carpet the stomach in 25% of cases).[[4](#cit/section_3.4),[5](#cit/section_3.5)] The hallmark feature of both classic and attenuated FAP is colonic polyposis followed by duodenal polyposis. In GAPPS, affected individuals notably lack duodenal and colorectal polyposis. For more information, see the [Familial Adenomatous Polyposis](/types/colorectal/hp/colorectal-genetics-pdq#_3205) section in Genetics of Colorectal Cancer.
- ***MUTYH*-associated polyposis (MAP)**. The gastric manifestations of MAP are attenuated when compared with those associated with FAP. However, more research on MAP-related gastric manifestations is necessary. For more information, see the [*MUTYH*-Associated Polyposis](/types/colorectal/hp/colorectal-genetics-pdq#_433) section in Genetics of Colorectal Cancer.
- **Proton pump inhibitor–induced gastric polyposis**. Sporadic fundic gland polyps can be seen in 3% to 5% of patients in the general population who use proton pump inhibitors.[[6](#cit/section_3.6)-[8](#cit/section_3.8)] These polyps tend to be isolated, are less than 5 mm in size, and do not show signs of dysplasia. For more information, see the [Introduction](/types/stomach/hp/gastric-genetics-pdq#_1340) section in Genetics of Gastric Cancer.
- **Hamartomatous polyposis syndromes**. The hamartomatous polyposis syndromes include *PTEN* tumor hamartoma syndromes, juvenile polyposis syndrome, and Peutz-Jeghers syndrome. Patients with these syndromes can present with gastric polyposis (including fundic gland polyps), although pathology usually includes hamartomatous polyps as well. Furthermore, gastric polyposis occurs in the antrum —an area of the stomach that is spared in individuals with GAPPS.[[1](#cit/section_3.1)] For more information, see the [*PTEN* hamartoma tumor syndromes (including Cowden syndrome)](/types/colorectal/hp/colorectal-genetics-pdq#_2559), [Peutz-Jeghers syndrome](/types/colorectal/hp/colorectal-genetics-pdq#_2920), and [Juvenile polyposis syndrome](/types/colorectal/hp/colorectal-genetics-pdq#_647) sections in Genetics of Colorectal Cancer.

## Clinical Features of GAPPS

### Gastric Manifestations in GAPPS

A key feature of gastric adenocarcinoma and proximal polyposis of the stomach (GAPPS) is extensive fundic gland polyposis in the proximal regions of the stomach with antral (distal) sparing. For more information, see the [Gastric polyps](/types/stomach/hp/gastric-genetics-pdq#_3753) section in Genetics of Gastric Cancer.[[1](#cit/section_4.1)] Since GAPPS is rare, literature on this syndrome primarily exists in the form of case reports and case series.[[2](#cit/section_4.2)-[6](#cit/section_4.6)] The incidence of gastric adenocarcinoma (intestinal or mixed types) in patients with GAPPS is 12% to 25% with age of diagnosis ranging from 23 years to 75 years.[[5](#cit/section_4.5)-[7](#cit/section_4.7)] Fundic gland polyposis (which precedes gastric adenocarcinoma) has occurred as early as 8 years in patients with GAPPS.[[8](#cit/section_4.8)] Gastric polyps range in size, with most polyps measuring less than 1 cm.[[5](#cit/section_4.5)-[7](#cit/section_4.7)] However, in GAPPS, gastric polyps can grow up to 4 cm in size. These polyps can be densely packed with extensive carpeting that covers the gastric mucosa without any visible normal mucosa. Most gastric polyps in GAPPS have fundic gland pathology with or without dysplasia. However, other pathologies are also seen, including hyperplastic polyps, adenomas, and hyperproliferative aberrant crypts. In a histological review of 25 patients, 15 patients had abnormal endoscopic biopsies.[[6](#cit/section_4.6)] Of these 15 individuals, 10 had hyperproliferative aberrant crypts, 10 had fundic gland polyps, 6 had gastric adenomas, 1 had an adenoma associated with gastric adenocarcinoma, and 1 had gastric adenocarcinoma. The [penetrance](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=339344&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) of fundic gland polyposis and gastric adenocarcinoma can vary between different families and among family members with the same *APC* [pathogenic variant](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=783960&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic). In a study of 24 Czech patients from 8 families (all had the same [germline](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=460154&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) c.-191T>C *APC* promotor 1B [variant](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=776887&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic)), some developed gastric polyposis in their 20s while others had not yet developed polyposis at age 65 years.[[5](#cit/section_4.5)] Five individuals in this study developed gastric adenocarcinoma between the ages of 29 years and 64 years.

### Colorectal Manifestations in GAPPS

Individuals with GAPPS may also have a propensity to develop colon polyps, although polyps are not thought to develop at an earlier age than those seen in the general population.[[7](#cit/section_4.7),[9](#cit/section_4.9)] It has been postulated that *APC*'s promoter 1A compensates for promoter 1B's dysfunction in the colonic mucosa, likely sparing the colorectum of polyposis. However, promoter 1A is methylated in the gastric mucosa, resulting in isolated gastric polyposis.[[10](#cit/section_4.10)] Nevertheless, GAPPS is caused by pathogenic variants in the same [gene](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45693&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) (*APC*) that causes familial adenomatous polyposis and can therefore, theoretically increase risk of colonic neoplasia.

Clinicopathologic evaluation of two families with GAPPS [phenotypes](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=460203&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) (based on clinical criteria) demonstrated that seven of nine affected individuals had colon pathology.[[9](#cit/section_4.9)] In contrast, the six [first-degree relatives](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=460150&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) (FDRs) in the study without GAPPS diagnoses had normal colonoscopies. In this study, the average age of individuals with GAPPS was 43.9 years (range, 20–71 y), whereas the average age of FDRs without GAPPS was 32.2 years (range, 18–51 y). In the GAPPS group, four of nine individuals had tubular adenomas in the colon, the youngest of whom was 48 years old. The colorectal polyps in individuals with GAPPS had increased beta-catenin, Ki67, and p53 expression. This expression pattern was also noted in the fundic gland polyps of these individuals, suggesting that GAPPS may be responsible. In the first paper that described GAPPS, 13 individuals with GAPPS underwent colonoscopy, in which six individuals (age, 18–37 y) did not have polyps, three individuals (age, 31–56 y) had tubular adenomas (including a 46-year-old individual who developed eight tubular adenomas over 4 years), and four individuals had hyperplastic polyps (one was located in the proximal colon, and the rest were located in the rectosigmoid colon).[[7](#cit/section_4.7)] Notably, none of the individuals had colonic polyposis. Colonoscopy findings were available for eight family members who did not have GAPPS. Three of these individuals (age, 55–77 y) had a single tubular adenoma with or without villous features. The other five individuals were less than 45 years old (age, 29–36 y) and had normal colonoscopies. In contrast to these studies, a report from eight French families with an *APC* pathogenic variant in promoter 1B revealed colonic polyposis in multiple members, which required colectomy.[[10](#cit/section_4.10),[11](#cit/section_4.11)] When these data are taken together, it is unclear whether individuals with GAPPS develop colonic neoplasia any earlier than individuals in the general population. However, there appears to be a trend in which individuals with GAPPS develop more polyps than individuals in the general population.

## Management for GAPPS

Consensus guidelines for management of gastric adenocarcinoma and proximal polyposis of the stomach (GAPPS) do not exist, given the rarity of this syndrome and the dearth of GAPPS literature (aside from case reports or case series). Furthermore, the [incomplete penetrance](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=792352&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) of GAPPS and its large age range of polyposis onset/gastric adenocarcinoma incidence makes creating uniform management recommendations challenging. The literature frequently recommends that providers use an individualized management approach for patients with GAPPS, with frequent upper endoscopic [surveillance](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=496506&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) and extensive sampling of gastric polyps.[[1](#cit/section_5.1)] The literature also suggests discussing gastrectomy planning early with a multidisciplinary team.

The goal of gastric endoscopic surveillance is to detect morphological or histological changes that increase risk for gastric adenocarcinoma. Generally, the detection of fundic gland dysplasia in GAPPS prompts heightened gastric cancer surveillance with more frequent endoscopic [screening](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46171&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) and discussions about risk-reducing gastrectomy with a multidisciplinary team. However, the natural progression in histology from fundic gland polyp to adenocarcinoma (including the precursor lesion to adenocarcinoma) is unknown. It has been hypothesized that hyperproliferative crypts found on pathology may be precursors to fundic gland polyps and adenomas in patients with GAPPS.[[2](#cit/section_5.2)] In addition to understanding the histological precursors of gastric adenocarcinoma, endoscopically identifying them is challenging, since they may easily be hidden or missed in the setting of dense polyp carpeting. For example, in a study, four members of a European family had GAPPS and developed gastric adenocarcinoma while under endoscopic surveillance.[[3](#cit/section_5.3)] In two of these individuals, gastric adenocarcinoma was identified in their gastrectomy specimens.

Given the variable [penetrance](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=339344&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) of GAPPS, the correlation between gastric adenocarcinoma and clinical features (such as age, [family history](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=302456&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic), degree of polyposis, and pathology of polyps) is unknown. However, given the high incidence of gastric adenocarcinoma seen in those with GAPPS (even when patients participate in gastric surveillance), discussion about risk-reducing gastrectomy with a multidisciplinary team may be warranted. Risk-reducing gastrectomy has been recommended when dysplasia is present.[[1](#cit/section_5.1),[4](#cit/section_5.4),[5](#cit/section_5.5)]

There are no consensus guidelines for colorectal surveillance in patients with GAPPS. Therefore, patients are managed based on their family histories of colorectal polyps/cancer with consideration of a baseline colonoscopy to exclude colonic polyposis.[[6](#cit/section_5.6)]

[Level of evidence: 5](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=526280&version=HealthProfessional&language=English)
