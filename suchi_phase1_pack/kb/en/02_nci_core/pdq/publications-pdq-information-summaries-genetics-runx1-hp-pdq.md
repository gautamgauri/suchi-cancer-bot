# RUNX1-familial Platelet Disorder (RUNX1-FPD) (PDQ®) - NCI

# RUNX1-Familial Platelet Disorder (PDQ®)–Health Professional Version

## Introduction and Clinical Manifestations of RUNX1–Familial Platelet Disorder (RUNX1-FPD)

RUNX1 (Runt-related [transcription](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=390290&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) factor 1) is a key transcription factor involved in hematopoietic differentiation, and it is one of the most commonly mutated [genes](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45693&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) in myeloid malignancies.[[1](#cit/section_1.1)]

[Inherited](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=809371&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) [pathogenic variants](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=783960&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) in the *RUNX1* gene lead to an [autosomal dominant](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=793860&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) [hereditary cancer syndrome](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=754220&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) called *RUNX1*-[familial](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=460148&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) platelet disorder (*RUNX1*-FPD) (also referred to as FPD with a propensity towards myeloid malignancies [FPD/MM] or acute myeloid leukemia [FPD/AML]).[[2](#cit/section_1.2)] Familial platelet functional defects have been clinically recognized for several decades.[[3](#cit/section_1.3)] In 1999, *RUNX1* was the first gene found to be associated with FPD/MM. *RUNX1*-FPD was the first germline predisposition syndrome associated with myeloid malignancies. In 2016, the World Health Organization (WHO) incorporated germline *RUNX1* pathogenic variants under a newly created subcategory of myeloid neoplasms called, “myeloid neoplasms with germline predisposition and preexisting platelet disorders.”[[4](#cit/section_1.4)] The WHO's inclusion of *RUNX1*-FPD in its classification guidelines has increased public awareness of this disorder.

Germline *RUNX1* pathogenic variants leading to *RUNX1*-FPD are associated with the following:[[5](#cit/section_1.5)]

- Lifetime mild to moderate thrombocytopenia associated with aspirin-like functional platelet defects.
- Approximately a 44% lifetime risk of developing myelodysplastic syndrome (MDS), AML, or T-cell acute lymphoblastic leukemia (T-ALL).

Although these are the most commonly reported hematologic cancers seen in individuals with *RUNX1*-FPD, other cancers have also been reported, including B-cell malignancies and myeloproliferative neoplasms.[[6](#cit/section_1.6)-[9](#cit/section_1.9)] There is a high [penetrance](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=339344&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) of thrombocytopenia and underlying platelet dysfunction in individuals with *RUNX1*-FPD. Thrombocytopenia is often recognized during childhood, although it has [variable expressivity](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=339331&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic), even within affected families. Hematologic malignancies are diagnosed at a median age of 33 years. However, the reported age range for an MDS or leukemia diagnosis is broad (range, 5 y to 76 y).[[5](#cit/section_1.5)] [Genetic anticipation](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=660750&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) (which can lead to disease manifestations occurring at earlier ages in successive generations) has also been described in *RUNX1*-FPD.[[9](#cit/section_1.9),[10](#cit/section_1.10)]

More than 200 families with germline *RUNX1* pathogenic variants have been described in the literature.[[11](#cit/section_1.11)] However, it is estimated that 5,515 families with *RUNX1* pathogenic variants exist worldwide, based on population incidence and a survey conducted by FPD/AML centers of excellence.[[12](#cit/section_1.12)] Importantly, a *RUNX1* database (RUNX1db) and registry is now available as a public resource.[[13](#cit/section_1.13)]  *RUNX1* [somatic variants](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=781854&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) have been detected in approximately 10% of AML cases, and the vast majority of these variants are somatic in nature. However, some studies have suggested that in an AML cohort, as many as 16% of *RUNX1*  variants that were identified by somatic testing could be germline in nature.[[14](#cit/section_1.14),[15](#cit/section_1.15)]

Phenotypic criteria proposed by the ClinGen Myeloid Malignancy Variant Curation Expert Panel (MM-VCEP) state that an individual should exhibit at least one of the following to fit the FPD/AML phenotype:[[12](#cit/section_1.12)]

1. Mild to moderate thrombocytopenia with normal platelet size and volume (in the absence of other causative factors like autoimmune- or drug-related thrombocytopenias).
2. Platelet ultra-structural and/or functional defects, including platelet alpha granule secretion defects, platelet dense granule secretion defects, and impaired platelet aggregation.
3. Diagnosis of a hematologic malignancy (most commonly affecting the myeloid lineage and manifesting as AML or MDS, or less frequently involving the lymphoid lineage and manifesting as T-ALL or other cancers).

In addition to thrombocytopenia and hematologic malignancies, *RUNX1*-FPD may also be associated with the following features: eczema, psoriasis, arthritis, and other autoimmune disorders.[[11](#cit/section_1.11)]

## Genetics and Molecular Biology of RUNX1-Familial Platelet Disorder (FPD)

Many of the [germline](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=460154&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) *RUNX1* [pathogenic variants](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=783960&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) seem to be unique to [probands](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=460211&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) or families.[[1](#cit/section_2.1)] The majority of *RUNX1* germline pathogenic [variants](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=776887&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) described in the literature are inherited, and [de novo](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=783882&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) germline *RUNX1* variants have been rarely reported.[[2](#cit/section_2.2)-[4](#cit/section_2.4)] The following types of germline pathogenic variants have been reported in the *RUNX1* [gene](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45693&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic): [missense variants](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=783964&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic), [nonsense variants](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=783965&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic), [splice-site variants](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=783968&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic), [single nucleotide variants](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=803525&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic), small [insertions](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=781204&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic), small [deletions](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=460141&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic), and [copy number variants](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=600383&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) (including intragenic and whole-gene deletions).[[3](#cit/section_2.3),[5](#cit/section_2.5),[6](#cit/section_2.6)] Pathogenic variants can happen at any location along the *RUNX1* gene, but these variants are most often located within the conserved runt-homology [domain](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=460145&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) (RHD) or the transactivation domain (TAD).[[7](#cit/section_2.7)]

[Genomic heterogeneity](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=339333&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) (in terms of somatic clonal evolution) is common, even among asymptomatic individuals with germline *RUNX1* pathogenic variants.[[7](#cit/section_2.7)-[9](#cit/section_2.9)] More than 80% of evaluated individuals with *RUNX1*-FPD demonstrate clonal evolution by age 50 years. Secondary [somatic variants](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=781854&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) in genes involved in [epigenetic](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=717443&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) regulation (i.e., *TET2* and *DNMT3A*) comprise most [variants](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=776887&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) identified in individuals with *RUNX1*-FPD who are undergoing [surveillance](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=496506&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic). These secondary variants are commonly associated with age-related clonal hematopoiesis. Many individuals with *RUNX1*-FPD–associated hematologic malignancies have evidence of [cytogenetic](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=270737&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) abnormalities (e.g., [trisomy](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=460223&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) 21, trisomy 8, monosomy 7). These individuals can also have secondary somatic variants in active signaling pathways (i.e., RAS pathway, KIT pathway) or in [tumor suppressor genes](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46657&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) (i.e., *WT1*, *PHF6*, *TP53*). Individuals can also have additional somatic variants in the *RUNX1* gene.[[10](#cit/section_2.10)]

Notably, baseline morphological and immunophenotypic abnormalities are often present in the bone marrow of asymptomatic individuals with *RUNX1*-FPD who do not have myelodysplastic syndrome (MDS)/acute myeloid leukemia.[[11](#cit/section_2.11),[12](#cit/section_2.12)] These abnormalities can include low-for-age bone marrow cellularity and dysplastic megakaryopoiesis, which is often characterized by small, hypolobated megakaryocytes. When mild dysplasia is already present, it can be a challenge to define when an individual's condition progresses to MDS.

## Management and Prognosis for RUNX1-Familial Platelet Disorder (FPD)

[Surveillance](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=496506&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) recommendations for individuals with *RUNX1*-FPD are based on expert opinion and typically include blood work (including a complete blood count [CBC] with differential conducted once or twice annually), in addition to a physical exam.[[1](#cit/section_3.1)] A bone marrow evaluation is suggested when an individual is first diagnosed with *RUNX1*-FPD. It is suggested that bone marrow evaluation be repeated when changes are found in the individual’s blood counts or when new, concerning medical findings (such as recurrent infections) occur. While annual bone marrow evaluations are often recommended for research purposes, there are no current clinical or pathological criteria that can reliably predict the development of hematologic malignancies in individuals with *RUNX1*-FPD.

Clinical outcomes and management decisions for individuals with *RUNX1*-FPD who experience malignant transformation are rarely reported. In general, standard treatment for the underlying malignancy (i.e., myelodysplastic syndrome, acute myeloid leukemia, or T-cell acute lymphoblastic leukemia directed-therapies) and consideration of an allogeneic stem cell transplant are recommended. Potential related donors should be evaluated for the [familial](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=460148&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) *RUNX1* [pathogenic variant](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=783960&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) to avoid transplanting stem cells with the same underlying genetic defect whenever possible. This approach can help avoid risks of graft failure and donor-derived leukemias.

## About This PDQ Summary

### Purpose of This Summary

This PDQ cancer information summary for health professionals provides comprehensive, peer-reviewed, evidence-based information about RUNX1-familial platelet disorder (RUNX1-FPD). It is intended as a resource to inform and assist clinicians in the care of their patients. It does not provide formal guidelines or recommendations for making health care decisions.

### Reviewers and Updates

This summary is reviewed regularly and updated as necessary by the [PDQ Cancer Genetics Editorial Board](https://www.cancer.gov/publications/pdq/editorial-boards/genetics "https://www.cancer.gov/publications/pdq/editorial-boards/genetics"), which is editorially independent of the National Cancer Institute (NCI). The summary reflects an independent review of the literature and does not represent a policy statement of NCI or the National Institutes of Health (NIH).

Board members review recently published articles each month to determine whether an article should:

- be discussed at a meeting,
- be cited with text, or
- replace or update an existing article that is already cited.

Changes to the summaries are made through a consensus process in which Board members evaluate the strength of the evidence in the published articles and determine how the article should be included in the summary.

The lead reviewers for RUNX1-Familial Platelet Disorder are:

- Julia Cooper, MS, CGC (Ohio State University)
- Courtney DiNardo, MD, MSC (University of Texas, M.D. Anderson Cancer Center)
- Marcin Wlodarski, MD, PhD (St. Jude Children's Research Hospital)

Any comments or questions about the summary content should be submitted to Cancer.gov through the NCI website's [Email Us](https://www.cancer.gov/contact/email-us "https://www.cancer.gov/contact/email-us"). Do not contact the individual Board Members with questions or comments about the summaries. Board members will not respond to individual inquiries.
