# PC-SPES - Complementary and Alternative Medicine (PDQ®) - NCI

# PC-SPES (PDQ®)–Health Professional Version

## Overview

[Go to Patient Version](/about-cancer/treatment/cam/patient/pc-spes-pdq)

**NOTE: There is either no new research on this topic or the recent published research is weak and not appropriate for inclusion in the summary. Therefore, the information in this summary is no longer being updated and is provided for reference purposes only.**

This [cancer](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45333&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) information summary provides an overview of the use of [PC-SPES](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=527377&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) as a treatment in people with cancer. The summary includes a brief history of PC-SPES research, the results of [clinical trials](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45961&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), and possible [adverse effects](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44922&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) of PC-SPES. Included in this summary is a discussion of the contamination of PC-SPES and its withdrawal from avenues of distribution.

This summary contains the following key information:

- PC-SPES is a patented mixture of eight herbs.
- PC-SPES was sold as a [dietary supplement](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=373932&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) to support and promote healthy [prostate](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46539&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) function.
- Each herb used in PC-SPES has been reported to have [anti-inflammatory](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44187&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), [antioxidant](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=43997&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), or [anticarcinogenic](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44272&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) properties.
- PC-SPES was recalled and withdrawn from the market because certain batches were contaminated with Food and Drug Administration–controlled [prescription](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44711&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) [drugs](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=348921&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov).
- The manufacturer is no longer in operation, and PC-SPES is no longer being made.
- There is evidence from both [laboratory](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44512&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) and [animal studies](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=454774&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) to suggest that PC-SPES had some effect in inhibiting prostate cancer [cell](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46476&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) growth and [prostate-specific antigen (PSA)](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46540&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) expression, but it is not known whether these results were caused by [adulterants](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=686042&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) such as [diethylstilbestrol](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46021&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), which is an [estrogenic](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46076&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) compound, the herbs in PC-SPES, or their combination.
- Evidence from clinical trials has shown that PC-SPES lowers PSA and [testosterone](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45581&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) levels in humans, but it is not known whether these results were caused by adulterants, the herbs in PC-SPES, or their combination.
- There is some evidence to suggest that PC-SPES has some anticancer effects that are not related to estrogen-like activity.
- Although there are products that claim to be substitutes for PC-SPES, they are not the patented original formulation. Few of these products have been the subject of [laboratory](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44512&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) or clinical trials reported in the peer-reviewed medical literature.

## General Information

Note: Separate PDQ summaries on [Prostate Cancer, Nutrition, and Dietary Supplements](/about-cancer/treatment/cam/hp/prostate-supplements-pdq) and [Prostate Cancer Treatment](/types/prostate/hp/prostate-treatment-pdq) are also available.

[PC-SPES](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=527377&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) is a patented herbal mixture that was sold as a [dietary supplement](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=373932&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) and used as a [complementary and alternative medicine (CAM)](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44964&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) treatment for [prostate](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46539&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) [cancer.](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45333&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) It is a combination of the following eight herbs:

- Baikal skullcap (*Scutellaria baicalensis* Georgi).
- Chrysanthemum (*Dendranthema morifolium*  [Ramat.] Tzvelev [synonym *Chrysanthemum morifolium*]).
- Ganoderma (*Ganoderma lucidum* [Curtis:fr] Karst.).
- Isatis (*Isatis indigotica* Fort.).
- Licorice (*Glycyrrhiza glabra*  L. or *Glycyrrhiza uralensis* Fisch. ex DC.).
- *Panax [ginseng](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44151&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov)* C.A. Meyer or pseudoginseng *(Panax pseudoginseng var. notoginseng* Hoo & tseng [synonym *Panax notoginseng*(Burkill)] F.H.Chen).
- *Isodon rubescens*  (Hemsl.) Hara (synonym *Rabdosia rubescens* [Hemsl.] Hara).
- Saw palmetto (*Serenoa repens* [Bartr.] Small).

With the exception of saw palmetto, the herbs in PC-SPES have been used individually or in combination in [Traditional Chinese Medicine](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=449722&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) (TCM) for a variety of health problems, including those of the prostate, for hundreds of years.[[1](#cit/section_2.1),[2](#cit/section_2.2)]

PC-SPES is an herbal product that resulted from a collaboration between a chemist at the New York Medical College in Valhalla, New York, and a Chinese herbalist and doctor of TCM in China. Their idea was to combine TCM with the scientific techniques of Western laboratory research. In the United States, a series of *[in vitro](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45733&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov)* and *[in vivo](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46352&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov)* [laboratory studies](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44512&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) was started on the mixture of herbs used in TCM specially formulated to treat prostate problems. Researchers published the results of these studies, which showed promising anticancer activity from PC-SPES.[[3](#cit/section_2.3)-[11](#cit/section_2.11)]

Considerable research has been conducted on the anticancer properties of the eight individual botanicals in PC-SPES.

Baikal skullcap (*Scutellaria baicalensis*)—Chinese name huang qin—contains baicalin and wogonin, two active flavones. Baicalin converts to baicalein, which is another active flavone. *In vitro*, baicalin and baicalein inhibit cell growth of AD LNCaP and JCA-1 AI human prostate cancer cell lines,[[12](#cit/section_2.12),[13](#cit/section_2.13)] as well as inducing [apoptosis](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46524&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) in human LNCaP cells.[[14](#cit/section_2.14)] Baicalin also shows antimutagenic and [antioxidant](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=43997&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) activity *in vitro* as well as [free radical](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44030&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) scavenging ability.[[15](#cit/section_2.15)-[20](#cit/section_2.20)]

Licorice (*Glycyrrhiza glabra* or *Glycyrrhiza uralensis*)—Chinese name gan cao—contains the very active [flavonoid](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=330168&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) licochalcone A, which has demonstrated *in vitro* estrogenic activity.[[21](#cit/section_2.21)] This [botanical](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=367458&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) shows a broad range of anticancer activity *in vitro*. It enhances the cytotoxicity of commonly used anticancer drugs and induces apoptosis in MCF-7 human [breast](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=304766&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) cancer and HL-60 [promyelocytic leukemia](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46159&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) cell lines.[[21](#cit/section_2.21)-[24](#cit/section_2.24)]

Reishi mushroom (*Ganoderma lucidum* [Curtis:
fr.] Karst.)—Chinese name ling zhi—
has been shown to aid in the recovery of [leukocyte](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46067&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) counts in [irradiated](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44836&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) mice in a [dose-dependent](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44110&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) manner. It contains the [polysaccharide](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44162&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) G009, which has demonstrated antioxidant behavior against HL-60 cells *in vitro* and dose-dependent inhibition of [lipid](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46068&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) peroxidation in rat brain cells *in vitro*.[[25](#cit/section_2.25)-[29](#cit/section_2.29)]

Isatis (*Isatis indigotica*)—Chinese name da qing ve—contains active agents in each part of the plant.[[2](#cit/section_2.2)] TCM has different names for the medicinals coming from the leaf, stem, and root and uses these plant products for different purposes. Indirubin, an active ingredient, and its [analogs](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44919&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) have demonstrated inhibition of cyclin-dependent kinases in human [mammary](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44560&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) [carcinoma](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45963&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) cell line MCF-7 *in vitro*.[[30](#cit/section_2.30)]

Ginseng (*Panax ginseng* or *Panax pseudoginseng var. notoginseng*)—Chinese name tianqi—contains ginsenosides and saponins. Of the 30 ginsenosides that have been isolated from *Panax ginseng*, only the 20(S)-protopanaxadiol type R3 has inhibited cell growth and suppressed [PSA](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44867&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) expression, [androgen](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45592&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) [receptor](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44958&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) and 5-alpha-reductase activity, and PCNA production *in vitro*.[[31](#cit/section_2.31)-[33](#cit/section_2.33)]

Chrysanthemum flowers (*Dendranthema morifolium*)—Chinese name ju hua—contain triterpene diols and triols. Arnidiol exhibited cytotoxicity *in vitro* against 58 of the 60 human cancer cell lines developed by the [National Cancer Institute](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44266&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) (NCI) Developmental Therapeutics Program.[[34](#cit/section_2.34)]

The botanical rabdosia rubescens (*Isodon rubescens*)—Chinese name dong ling cao—has two very active agents, oridonin and rubesencin b. Oridonin inhibits [DNA](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45671&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) synthesis *in vitro*,[[1](#cit/section_2.1)] and rubesencin b inhibited cell growth in cancer cell lines *in vitro* and in a mouse model.[[35](#cit/section_2.35)]

Saw palmetto (*Serenoa repens*) is the only botanical in PC-SPES that is not used in TCM. There is strong evidence from human trials that saw palmetto has some activity against [benign prostatic hypertrophy](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44372&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) (BPH), including improved [urine](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46642&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) flow and less [erectile dysfunction](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=321372&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) when compared with [placebo](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46688&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) or [finasteride.](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45336&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) *S. repens* also exhibits antiestrogenic activity in [placebo-controlled](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44840&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) BPH trials. In LNCaP cells, *S. repens* produced apoptosis *in vitro*.[[36](#cit/section_2.36)-[40](#cit/section_2.40)]

Exactly how PC-SPES works in the body is still unknown. The presence of [adulterants](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=686042&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) and varying amounts of the active agents in each lot of PC-SPES complicates the interpretation of any results from studies that might lead to an explanation of its mechanisms of action. More studies of the individual components of the mixture and testing of a standard formulation that is free of adulterants are needed before any conclusions can be reached about the level of cytotoxicity, antineoplasticity, or estrogenicity of PC-SPES.

The [National Center for Complementary and Integrative Health](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=454777&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) (NCCIH) stopped funding to studies of PC-SPES after the drug contamination was detected and made public, although the laboratory studies were later resumed.

Although manufacturers are selling supplements purporting to be substitutes, the only company that had a license from the patent holder to manufacture PC-SPES is no longer in business, and the product cannot be legally manufactured in the United States without the patent holder’s permission. PC-SPES is not legally available in the United States.

## History

In 1997, the herbal formula for [PC-SPES](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=527377&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) was patented in the United States.[[1](#cit/section_3.1)] A company, BotanicLab (Brea, California), was formed to produce, distribute, and sell the product. PC-SPES was sold through the BotanicLab website (the website was taken down after PC-SPES was recalled) and through selected distributors. Anecdotal information about the product and its positive effects was widely circulated on the Internet through websites that informed [prostate cancer](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=445079&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) patients about new developments in treatment. At the same time, the published papers were being read by the scientific community, and the findings were presented at various conferences. As a result, clinicians and researchers began looking at PC-SPES as one of the first viable treatments to come out of the [alternative medicine](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44921&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) community.

The manufacturing process for PC-SPES has been described by the manufacturer as follows: [extracts](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=407760&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) of raw plant material were obtained from the specified plants, which were grown in particular geographic regions in China and harvested at certain times of the year to reduce the natural variation inherent in any [biological](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44510&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) product. The extracts were shipped to the United States, where high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) was used to monitor the key active compounds—which are believed to be directly related to the [clinical](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44168&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) effects—for batch-to-batch reproducibility. Activity-related [biomarkers](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45618&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) were kept in a constant concentration from lot to lot. A commercial testing laboratory (Truesdail Laboratory; Tustin, California) was used to guarantee that each batch was free from contamination with heavy metals, pesticides, [microorganisms](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44059&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) and products, and [prescription](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44711&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) [drugs](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=348921&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov). Each lot was standardized by an anticancer bioassay for an effective [dose](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44664&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) of 50% *in vitro* inhibition of [cell](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46476&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) growth using human LNCaP cells for androgen-dependent (AD) prostate cancer and [DU-145](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=390267&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) cells for [androgen-independent](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=350224&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) (AI) prostate cancer. The powder was then encapsulated, bottled, labeled, and sterilized at the BotanicLab facility.[[2](#cit/section_3.2)]

In 2001, allegations that PC-SPES contained the synthetic [estrogen](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46076&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) [diethylstilbestrol](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46021&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) (DES) started to appear on e-mail listservs used by prostate cancer patients and in online newsletters. Prostate cancer patients who were taking PC-SPES noticed that their recent medication was not as effective as the previous batches.[[3](#cit/section_3.3)] A sample of PC-SPES submitted to a testing laboratory by BotanicLab in August 2001 found no [DES](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44873&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov). BotanicLab posted the letter from the laboratory on their website, claiming that PC-SPES contained no DES. However, in other tests of six different lots of PC-SPES received from two different sources in August 2001, Rocky Mountain Instrumental Laboratory found varying amounts of DES in three lots. More tests done by the California Department of Health Services in February 2002 did not find DES but did find [warfarin](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45249&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), a prescription drug used as a [blood thinner](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44371&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov).[[4](#cit/section_3.4)]

The presence of a synthetic estrogen such as DES was suspected early in the clinical use of PC-SPES after reports in the literature discussed the mixture’s estrogen-like ability to lower [prostate-specific antigen (PSA)](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46540&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) levels in AD prostate cancer patients. In addition, the [side effects](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46580&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) of treatment were similar to those of estrogen [therapy.](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44737&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) [[5](#cit/section_3.5)-[7](#cit/section_3.7)] In one study, patients who showed the most [response](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44085&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) to PC-SPES were also those who were the most responsive to DES.[[8](#cit/section_3.8),[9](#cit/section_3.9)] The same study also attempted to find out whether DES or similar compounds were present in PC-SPES. Transcriptional activation assays in yeast strain PL3 *Saccharomyces cerevisiae* using an ethanolic extract of PC-SPES showed estrogenic activity similar to 1nM [estradiol](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=305990&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov). In addition, ovariectomized CD-1 mice showed substantially increased [uterine](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46645&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) weights. HPLC, gas chromatography, and mass spectrometry did not reveal the presence of DES but rather that of a compound with similar chemical characteristics. The authors of the report concluded that PC-SPES contains estrogenic compounds that are distinct from DES or other synthetic estrogens.[[9](#cit/section_3.9)]

A definitive evaluation of PC-SPES analyzed specific lots of PC-SPES capsules manufactured from 1996 to 2001.[[10](#cit/section_3.10)] In addition to using HPLC to isolate, identify, and quantify the synthetic drugs and active [phytoestrogens](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=330175&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), this study also identified components using [proton](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44715&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) nuclear magnetic resonance, gas chromatography/mass spectrometry, and mass spectra [analysis](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=390238&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov). Tests showed the presence of the synthetic drugs [indomethacin](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45223&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) (a [nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drug](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44481&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) not previously reported in the literature or found in other testing), DES, and warfarin. Testing was also
done for concentrations of the two naturally occurring phytosterols, licochalcone A and baicalin. Test results indicated a history of rising and falling levels of contamination by the three synthetic drugs and a recent rise in the naturally occurring phytochemicals in PC-SPES. Lots of PC-SPES manufactured in 1996 through mid-1999 contained indomethacin ranging from 1.07 mg/g to 13.19 mg/g and DES ranging from 107.28 µg/g to 159.27 µg/g and were 2 to 6 times more [antineoplastic](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44816&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) and up to 50 times more estrogenic than lots manufactured after the spring of 1999. *In vitro* testing of ethanolic extracts of PC-SPES against LNCaP, PC-3, and DU-145 prostate cancer [cell lines](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44016&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) showed a decrease in both antineoplasticity and estrogenicity in lots of PC-SPES manufactured in June 1998 through August 2001, which correlated with the amount of DES and indomethacin contamination.[[10](#cit/section_3.10)] Another *in vitro* test of suspected lots of PC-SPES manufactured from 2000 to 2001 also showed the presence of DES.[[11](#cit/section_3.11)]

Although the laboratory testing showed that certain lots of the mixture contained indomethacin, warfarin, and DES, the amount of DES present may not have accounted for all of the estrogenic effect of PC-SPES. There is some evidence that the mixture acts differently from DES at the molecular level.[[12](#cit/section_3.12),[13](#cit/section_3.13)] In addition, its anticancer effects on both AI prostate cancer and AD prostate cancer may point to a mechanism other than estrogen-like activity.[[10](#cit/section_3.10),[14](#cit/section_3.14),[15](#cit/section_3.15)] The *in vitro* activity of PC-SPES against cancer cells other than prostate also gives rise to the speculation that its estrogen-like qualities might not account for all of the mixture’s anticancer activity.[[16](#cit/section_3.16),[17](#cit/section_3.17)]

[Burkill] F.H.Chen)
was next at 80.9% reduction, followed by *Glycyrrhiza uralensis* Fisch ex DC. (73%). The lowest reduction in cell growth was exhibited by *Serenoa repens* (Bartr.) Small
(14.5%). *Scutellaria baicalensis* Georgi, *Serenoa repens*, and *Glycyrrhiza uralensis* lowered [PSA](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44867&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) expression, but each of the other herbs increased PSA expression. The ability of individual herbs to reduce PSA expression was not uniform, but the PC-SPES mixture as a whole exhibited a uniform [response.](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44085&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) The varying results with the individual herbs and the positive response of the cells (i.e., increased cytotoxicity and reduced PSA expression) to the aggregate PC-SPES mixture may suggest that the botanicals in PC-SPES work in concert and that no individual herb can account for the overall effects of the mixture.[[1](#cit/section_4.1)]

In other studies, PC-SPES was found to inhibit clonal growth in three human [prostate](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46539&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) [cancer](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45333&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) [cell lines](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44016&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov): LNCaP, PC-3, and [DU-145](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=390267&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov). Cell cycle [analysis](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=390238&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) showed cell cycle arrest at the G2 phase.[[2](#cit/section_4.2)] [Cell proliferation](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46479&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) and reduced clonogenicity were observed in cancer cell lines other than those of prostate cancer: human [breast](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=304766&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) [carcinoma](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45963&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) lines MCF-7 and T47-D, SK-N-MC neuroepithelioma, COLO 38 [melanoma](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45135&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), U937 histiomonocytic [lymphoma](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45368&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), and HL-60 and MOLT-4 [leukemias](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45343&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov). [Cytotoxic](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44020&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) and cytostatic effects of PC-SPES were common to all [tumor](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46634&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) cell lines tested.[[3](#cit/section_4.3)]

In another study evaluating regulation of PSA expression and [androgen](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45592&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) [receptor](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44958&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) (AR) activity, LNCaP prostate cancer cell lines showed downregulation of both [proliferating](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44572&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) cell nuclear [antigen](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46086&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) (PCNA) and PSA expression. PSA changes occurred [concurrently](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44011&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) with the decrease of PCNA. The results suggest that PC-SPES modulates cell growth by changing PCNA expression and may decrease PSA levels indirectly by suppressing AR expression.[[4](#cit/section_4.4)]

None of the studies above indicated lot number or year of manufacture of the PC-SPES used. Therefore, it is not possible to assess the amount of contamination of the mixtures used in the studies or whether the mixtures used were not in fact contaminated.

A 1998 study that evaluated estrogenic activity of extracts of PC-SPES, ginseng (*Panax ginseng* C.A. Meyer), saw palmetto, DES, and estrone [(estradiol](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=305990&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov)-17 beta) *[in vitro](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45733&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov)* reported on the estrogenic response of ovariectomized CD-1 mice to PC-SPES [extract](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=407760&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) as well as the response to PC-SPES capsules in eight prostate cancer patients who had received previous [therapy.](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44737&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) [[5](#cit/section_4.5)] This study used four samples of PC-SPES ordered in separate purchases from BotanicLab. No lot numbers were supplied in the study. Lot numbers from October 1996 through July 1998 were later tested for contamination and had DES levels of 114.74 μg/g to 159.27 μg/g, as well as the highest detected levels of indomethacin of the PC-SPES lots tested.[[6](#cit/section_4.6)] *In vitro* tests of PC-SPES extract or estradiol showed estrogenic activity similar to 1 nM estradiol on [estrogen receptor](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46409&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) Y253 yeast strain. Transcriptional activation assays in yeast strain PL3 *Saccharomyces cerevisiae* with ethanolic extract of PC-SPES exhibited estrogen-like effects. In the eight prostate cancer patients, [serum](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44088&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) [testosterone](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45581&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) concentrations decreased during the use of PC-SPES and increased within 3 weeks after treatment was discontinued. PSA levels decreased in all eight patients. [Side effects](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46580&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) in all eight patients were similar to those seen after treatment with estrogen: breast tenderness and loss of libido. One patient had superficial venous [thrombosis](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44231&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov). In addition to baicalin, two other compounds purified from PC-SPES, isoliquiritigenin and wogonin, have been shown to reduce PSA levels and downregulate AR.[[7](#cit/section_4.7)]

By incorporating PC-SPES into the rat [diet](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44660&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), researchers conducting an *[in vivo](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46352&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov)* study showed antitumor effects using a Dunning R3327 rat prostate cancer model. Levels of 0.05% and 0.025% of dietary PC-SPES were fed to the rats over a 6-week period. No toxicity was seen, nor was there a difference in the food intake of the rats during this time. [Pulmonary](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45852&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) tumors were induced by [intradermal](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44602&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) [injections](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44678&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) of MAT-LyLu cells, which are particularly [resistant](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=416101&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) to many forms of treatment. Tumor [incidence](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46145&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) was inhibited in a dose-dependent manner, and the rate of tumor growth showed the same dose-dependent response.[[8](#cit/section_4.8),[9](#cit/section_4.9)]

In another study, which used male BNX nu/nu [immunodeficient](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46357&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) nude mice, PC-SPES was also administered [orally](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44068&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), but in suspension. The mice received 300 rad of [whole-body irradiation](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45930&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), after which they were inoculated with either PC-3 or DU-145 prostate cancer cell lines. Treatment with PC-SPES began the day after injection. Results showed that PC-SPES suppressed the growth of DU-145 tumors compared with tumor growth in the [control group](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44149&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov). Cytological analysis showed [apoptosis](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46524&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) in the treated group that was not apparent in the control group.[[10](#cit/section_4.10)]

In two other studies, [clinical studies](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44195&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) of patients were initiated along with *in vitro* and *in vivo* research. The results of these two patient groups are discussed in the [Human/Clinical Trials](/about-cancer/treatment/cam/hp/pc-spes-pdq#_37) section of this summary. The first study, preceding more extensive research, examined *in vitro* activity of PC-SPES against LNCaP, LNCaP-bcl-2, PC-3, and DU-145 cells lines. Results showed that PC-SPES was active in suppressing both [hormone](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45713&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov)-sensitive and hormone-insensitive prostate cancer cell lines. In the subsequent study, research was conducted *in vitro* on the ability of PC-SPES to induce apoptosis in [androgen-independent](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=350224&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) (AI) prostate cancer cell lines, and *in vivo* on the effect of oral PC-SPES on the growth of xenografted PC-3 tumors in immunodeficient male mice. Mice in the treatment arm—in which treatment was started 1 week after implantation—showed a [significant](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=390271&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) decrease in tumor weight when compared with mice in the control arm. PC-SPES showed activity against both [androgen](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45592&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov)-sensitive and AI prostate cancer in the patients and suppressed tumor growth in AI tumors in mice.[[10](#cit/section_4.10)-[12](#cit/section_4.12)] In both studies, the patients were given capsules manufactured between 1996 and 1999, a time when contamination levels of DES were highest.[[6](#cit/section_4.6)]

Another study in rats demonstrated that PC-SPES (one lot contaminated with DES and one lot without DES) caused some decrease in the activity of a [liver](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46312&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) [enzyme](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46081&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) involved in [drug](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=348921&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) [metabolism](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46173&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) (CYP3A).[[13](#cit/section_4.13)]

## Human/Clinical Studies

One published [randomized](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45858&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) cross-over study of patients with [androgen-independent](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=350224&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) (AI) prostate cancer who initially received either 960 mg of [PC-SPES](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=527377&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) 3 times a day or 3 mg of [diethylstilbestrol](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46021&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) (DES) once a day before crossing over to the other [regimen](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45864&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov); when disease progression occurred, there were reports of data demonstrating the presence and levels of [adulterants](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=686042&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) in the four lots of PC-SPES used in this trial. The lots were manufactured by BotanicLab (Brea, California). The study was halted and chemical analyses of the lots were performed. The analyses showed that all four lots of PC-SPES contained amounts of DES ranging from 0.1 μg/g to 32.7μg/g, and that one lot contained varying amounts of ethinyl estradiol. The authors concluded that the presence of these adulterants rendered their results inconclusive.[[1](#cit/section_5.1)]

Several nonrandomized [clinical studies](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44195&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) published between 1999 and 2003 described the results of clinical trials conducted before adulterants had been conclusively identified in PC-SPES lots and before it was known that there was [significant](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=390271&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) variation in naturally occurring active agents, such as baicalein and licochalcone-A, in the lots. These studies, many of which enrolled small numbers of patients, did not identify the source of the lots that were used in the trials, nor did they identify where patients acquired PC-SPES.

In addition to the confounding effects of adulterants on the clinical trial results discussed below, the fact that an optimal [dose](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44664&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) of PC-SPES remains undetermined and that dose varies among these studies makes it difficult to compare their findings.

In a [retrospective study](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44956&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) of 23 consecutive patients with AI disease, charts were evaluated for patients’ [responses](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44085&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) to PC-SPES and the occurrence of any toxicity. There is no report of where the patients acquired their PC-SPES or what lots were used. Patients were all seen between February and November in 1999. Patients ranged in age from 51 to 88 years, with a [median](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44941&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) age of 70 years. All had previous initial [androgen ablation](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44369&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) for a period of 6 months to 144 months. Ten patients had received [chemotherapy](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45214&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), 13 had not. More than half of the patients with AI showed a post-[therapy](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44737&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) [prostate-specific antigen (PSA)](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46540&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) decline of 50% or greater. Median time to [PSA](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44867&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) [progression](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44078&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) was 6 months. The [side effects](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46580&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) were similar to those of [estrogen](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46076&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) therapy (gynecomastia and [impotence](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=321376&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov)). Other side effects were nausea/vomiting and [diarrhea](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=306496&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) and to a lesser extent, allergic reactions, leg cramps, and leg swelling.[[2](#cit/section_5.2)]

In a [prospective](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44079&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) clinical trial of 16 men with [stage](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45885&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) D3 [metastatic](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44058&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) [prostate](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46539&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) cancer in which all patients had failed therapy and had [disease progression](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45669&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), the effects of PC-SPES on pain, [quality of life](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45417&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), and side effects were assessed. Previous therapy was either [orchidectomy](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=318820&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) or a luteinizing hormone–releasing [agonist](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46054&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) with or without [antiandrogen](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46052&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov). [Hormonal therapy](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46367&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) was continued throughout the trial to avoid the known withdrawal effect of antiandrogen on PSA levels. There was a significant decrease in pain scores such that the 14 patients on [analgesics](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45590&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) required an average of 40% less analgesics while taking PC-SPES. PC-SPES treatment was associated with improved function and emotional and physical well-being. PSA levels declined significantly after PC-SPES therapy (>50%). Side effects were [breast](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=304766&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) tenderness, deep venous [thrombosis](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44231&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), and mild dyspepsia.[[3](#cit/section_5.3),[4](#cit/section_5.4)]

In a study of 70 patients, 37 with [androgen](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45592&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov)-dependent (AD) disease and 33 with AI disease, the AD cohort was treated with PC-SPES only after an initial treatment with [prostatectomy](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45851&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), [radiation](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45072&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), [cryotherapy](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45985&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), and/or hormonal therapy. Median duration of PSA [response](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44085&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) was greater than 57 weeks. All patients in the AD cohort had PSA declines within a range of 80% to 100%, and two patients with [bone metastases](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45317&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) showed improvement on radiographic [analysis](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=390238&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov). Within the AI cohort, 54% (19 of 35) had a PSA decrease of greater than 50%, with median time to nadir of 10 weeks and a median duration of 18 weeks. Eight of the 16 patients who had received [ketoconazole](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45222&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) therapy prior to PC-SPES also obtained a decrease of greater than 50% in their PSA values. [Testosterone](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45581&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) levels within the AD group decreased to castrate levels (<50 ng/mL) in 94% of patients (31 of 33), and libido (25 of 25) and potency (15 of 15) were lost in all patients who entered the study. Side effects were [hot flashes](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=256567&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), gynecomastia/gynecodynia, and thromboembolic effects in 3 of 70 patients. Although the results of this trial were promising for the treatment of both AI and AD prostate cancer, it is not possible to assess what was responsible for these effects. This trial used PC-SPES from one single lot, but the published study does not indicate the lot number. The research was completed before 2000. No attempt was made to assess the possible contamination of the product.[[3](#cit/section_5.3),[5](#cit/section_5.5)]

A prospective [clinical series](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44010&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) assessed the ability of PC-SPES to lower [serum](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44088&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) PSA levels in 33 prostate cancer patients. The patients had either refused [conventional therapy](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44198&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) or had failed previous [cryosurgery](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45407&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), [radiation therapy](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44971&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), and/or hormonal therapy. No overt signs of disease progression were found in any of the patients. At 2 months, PSA levels had decreased by a [mean](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=285973&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) of 52% in 27 of the 31 patients and had increased in two patients. Of the five patients who had [hormone](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45713&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov)-[refractory disease](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45863&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), all had decreased serum PSA levels.[[3](#cit/section_5.3),[6](#cit/section_5.6)]

In a continuation of the previous study, a total of 69 patients with either AI or AD disease were separated into three study groups. Group one (n = 43) had undergone previous therapy, including hormonal; group two (n = 22) developed AI after treatment; and group three (n = 4) had not undergone previous therapy. The study assessed PC-SPES activity in suppressing PSA levels. Patients were given three capsules of PC-SPES 3 times per day. PSA levels and side effects were observed for 24 months.[[7](#cit/section_5.7)]

In group one, 82% of patients (32 of 39) had a decrease in PSA levels, with 20 patients having a decrease of greater than 50% at 2 months’ [follow-up](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44671&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov); the decrease lasted for 24 months in two patients. In group two (AI patients), 90% (19 of 21) had a decrease in PSA at their 2-month follow-up, with 66% (14 of 21) having a decrease of greater than 50% in PSA levels. At 24 months, two patients had a decrease of 20% to 50% in pretreatment PSA levels. In group three, 50% (2 of 4) had a decrease of greater than 50% in PSA levels at 2 months, and the remaining two patients had an increase at 2 and 6 months. Eighty-two percent of study patients had a decreased PSA level after 2 months of therapy. Side effects included [nipple](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=304735&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) tenderness (42%), gynecomastia (8%), hot flashes, and deep venous thrombosis.[[7](#cit/section_5.7)] In both Germany and the United Kingdom, PC-SPES–like formulations have been studied. A phase I trial of PC-Spes2 in the United Kingdom encountered tolerability problems due to diarrhea.[[8](#cit/section_5.8)]

## Summary of the Evidence for PC-SPES

To assist readers in evaluating the results of human studies of integrative, alternative, and complementary therapies for [cancer](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45333&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), the strength of the evidence (i.e., the [levels of evidence](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=446533&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov)) associated with each type of treatment is provided whenever possible. To qualify for a level of evidence [analysis](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=390238&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), a study must:

- Be published in a [peer-reviewed scientific journal](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=537399&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov).
- Report on [therapeutic](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=43985&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) [outcome](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=467853&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) or outcomes, such as [tumor](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46634&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) [response](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44085&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), improvement in survival, or measured improvement in [quality of life](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45417&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov).
- Describe [clinical](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44168&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) findings in sufficient detail that a meaningful evaluation can be made.

The lack of consistent composition of [PC-SPES](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=527377&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) due to varying concentrations of [adulterants](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=686042&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) makes it difficult to determine the effects of PC-SPES in humans; therefore, no level of evidence analysis is possible for this treatment. At this time, the use of PC-SPES as a treatment for cancer cannot be recommended outside the context of well-designed [clinical trials](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45961&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov).

For additional information about levels of evidence analysis, refer to [Levels of Evidence for Human Studies of Integrative, Alternative, and Complementary Therapies](/publications/pdq/levels-evidence/cam).
