# Acupuncture (PDQ®) - NCI

# Acupuncture (PDQ®)–Health Professional Version

## Overview

[Go to Patient Version](/about-cancer/treatment/cam/patient/acupuncture-pdq)

This [cancer](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45333&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) information summary provides an overview of the use of [acupuncture](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46724&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) as a treatment for individuals with cancer or cancer-related [disorders](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=407758&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov). The summary includes a brief history of acupuncture practice, a review of [laboratory](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44512&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) and [animal studies](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=454774&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), the results of [clinical](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44168&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) observations and [trials](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45961&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), and possible [side effects](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46580&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) of acupuncture [therapy](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44737&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov). Information presented in some sections of the summary can also be found in tables located at the end of those sections.

This summary contains the following key information:

- As part of [traditional Chinese medicine](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=449722&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), acupuncture has been practiced in China and other Asian countries for thousands of years.
- Acupuncture is defined as the application of stimulation such as [needling](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=449723&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), [moxibustion](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=449724&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), [cupping](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=449725&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), and [acupressure](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46723&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) on specific sites of the body known as [acupuncture points](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=449727&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov).
- Acupuncture has been practiced in the United States for about 200 years. The [U.S. Food and Drug Administration](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=454785&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) approved the [acupuncture needle](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=449728&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) as a [medical device](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=449732&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) in 1996.
- Acupuncture is used to treat a wide range of illnesses and ailments. Cancer patients use it for pain management, control of [nausea](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=390302&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) and [vomiting](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=390324&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) (N/V), [fatigue](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=321374&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), [hot flashes](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=256567&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), [xerostomia](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44537&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), [neuropathy](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46041&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), [anxiety](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=430405&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), [depression](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=430479&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), and sleep disturbance.
- Laboratory and animal studies exploring the mechanisms of acupuncture for cancer treatment have focused mainly on the role of acupuncture in the activation of neurohormonal pathways and [immunomodulation](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=367426&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov).
- The aim of most acupuncture clinical observation and clinical trials in cancer patients has been to evaluate the effects of acupuncture on [symptom management](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=269453&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov).
- The most convincing research data on the effects of acupuncture in cancer patients have emerged from studies of the management of [chemotherapy](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45214&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov)-induced N/V.

## General Information

[Acupuncture](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46724&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), a complementary [therapy](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44737&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) used in [symptom management](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=269453&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov),[[1](#cit/section_2.1)-[4](#cit/section_2.4)] is used [clinically](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44168&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) to manage [cancer](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45333&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov)-related [symptoms](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45022&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), treat [side effects](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46580&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) induced by anticancer therapies, boost [blood cell count](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44648&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), and enhance [lymphocyte](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45765&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) and [natural killer (NK) cell](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44439&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) activity. In cancer treatment, its primary use is symptom management; commonly treated symptoms are cancer pain,[[4](#cit/section_2.4),[5](#cit/section_2.5)] [chemotherapy](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45214&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov)-induced [nausea](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=390302&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) and [vomiting](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=390324&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) (N/V),[[6](#cit/section_2.6),[7](#cit/section_2.7)] and other symptoms that affect a patient’s [quality of life](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45417&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), including weight loss, [anxiety,](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=430405&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) [depression,](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=430479&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) [insomnia](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44043&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), poor [appetite,](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=454699&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) [fatigue](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=321374&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), [xerostomia](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44537&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), [hot flashes](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=256567&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), chemotherapy-induced [peripheral neuropathy](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44705&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), [gastrointestinal](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45692&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) symptoms [(constipation](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=407757&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) and [diarrhea](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=306496&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov)), and [postoperative](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=43980&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) [ileus](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=558995&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov).[[8](#cit/section_2.8)-[10](#cit/section_2.10)] Acupuncture is acceptable and safe for children.[[11](#cit/section_2.11),[12](#cit/section_2.12)] See [Pediatric Population and Acupuncture](/about-cancer/treatment/cam/hp/acupuncture-pdq#_460) for more information.

Cancer patients are receptive to receiving acupuncture for symptom control. A 2018 cross-sectional study of [breast cancer](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=444971&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) [survivors](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=450125&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) showed that an equal percentage of patients preferred acupuncture versus [medication](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=463717&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) for pain management.[[13](#cit/section_2.13)] A similar study in 2020 showed that fear of [analgesic](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45590&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) side effects is significantly associated with preference for acupuncture.[[14](#cit/section_2.14)] On the other hand, the most common barriers to using acupuncture for these patients were lack of knowledge about acupuncture, concerns about lack of insurance coverage, cost, and difficulty finding qualified acupuncturists. For acupuncture to become part of the [standard of care](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=346525&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) for cancer patients, further education about acupuncture, improved insurance coverage, and accessibility to qualified acupuncturists are needed.[[15](#cit/section_2.15)]

More than 40 states and the District of Columbia have laws regulating acupuncture practice. The National Certification Commission for Acupuncture and Oriental Medicine offers national certification examinations for [practitioners](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=463164&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) of acupuncture and [traditional Chinese medicine](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=449722&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) (TCM) ([www.nccaom.org](http://www.nccaom.org/ "http://www.nccaom.org/")); most, but not all, states require this certification. More than 50 schools and colleges of acupuncture and [Oriental medicine](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=449739&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) operate in the United States, many of which offer master’s-level programs and are accredited by or have been granted candidacy status by the Accreditation Commission for Acupuncture and Oriental Medicine (ACAOM). ACAOM standards for a master's-level degree require a 3-year program (approximately 2,000 hours of study) for acupuncture and a 4-year program for Oriental medicine, which includes acupuncture and [herbal therapy](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=462683&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) ([www.ACAOM.org](http://www.acaom.org/ "http://www.acaom.org/")). In recent years, some schools have begun to offer programs for Doctor of Acupuncture and Oriental Medicine with an additional 1,200 hours of clinical-based doctoral training. Some Western medical training, including the study of [anatomy](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=467828&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), physiology, and clean-needle technique, is included in the curriculums of these schools. Postgraduate training programs in medical acupuncture for [physicians](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=390246&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) also exist. In the United States, training to be a licensed [acupuncturist](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=613192&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) is regulated according to individual state law. Because the educational and licensing requirements for acupuncture practice vary from state to state, one should inquire from each state board of acupuncture (or other relevant board) for information ([www.nccaom.org](https://www.nccaom.org/ "https://www.nccaom.org/")). Third-party reimbursements also vary from state to state. Some insurance companies cover acupuncture or limited acupuncture treatment. Federal payers such as [Medicare](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=640052&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) and [Medicaid](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=640050&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) do not generally reimburse for acupuncture treatment.

Acupuncture has been practiced in China and other Asian countries for more than 4,000 years.[[16](#cit/section_2.16)-[18](#cit/section_2.18)] In China, acupuncture is part of a TCM system of traditional medical knowledge and is practiced along with other treatment [modalities](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=270868&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) such as herbal medicine, [tui na](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=454783&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) ([massage](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=454761&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) and [acupressure](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46723&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov)), [mind/body exercise](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=467850&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) (e.g., [qigong](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=467860&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) and [tai chi](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=467869&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov)), and dietary therapy.[[19](#cit/section_2.19),[20](#cit/section_2.20)] In the United States, several different acupuncture styles are practiced in addition to TCM. These include Japanese acupuncture (e.g., [meridian](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=449744&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) therapy), English acupuncture (e.g., [five-element or traditional acupuncture](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=467839&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov)), French acupuncture (e.g., French energetic acupuncture), [Korean acupuncture](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=485163&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) (e.g., [constitutional acupuncture](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=467834&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov)), and American medical acupuncture. Most of these styles are derived from ancient Chinese medical philosophy and practices. All are based on the view that the human body must be perceived and treated as a whole and as part of nature; health is the result of harmony among bodily functions and between the body and nature, and disease occurs when this harmony is disrupted. TCM [therapeutic](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=43985&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) [interventions](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=454757&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), including acupuncture, are used to restore the state of harmony.

Acupuncture is closely associated with [Chinese meridian theory](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=449742&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov). According to this theory, there are 12 primary meridians, or channels, and eight additional meridians, each following a particular directional course along the body. A [vital](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46649&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) energy known as [qi](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=454782&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) flows through these meridians and participates in the [homeostatic](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=467842&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) regulation of various bodily functions. Along the meridians are approximately 360 points that serve as both [pathognomonic](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=454769&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) [signs](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=750109&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) of [disorder](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=407758&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) and as [loci](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=460162&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) for acupuncture treatments.[[18](#cit/section_2.18),[21](#cit/section_2.21)] When the normal flow of energy over a meridian is obstructed (e.g., as a result of [tissue](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46683&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) injury or a [tumor](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46634&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov)), pain or other symptoms result. Chinese medicine proposes that the purpose of acupuncture therapy is to normalize energy flow, thereby relieving the symptoms by stimulating specific sites ([acupuncture points](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=449727&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov)) on the meridians.[[22](#cit/section_2.22)] In acupuncture treatment, stainless steel needles, usually ranging from 0.22 mm to 0.25 mm in
[diameter,](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44224&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) are inserted into relevant acupuncture points to stimulate the affected meridians. A [needling](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=449723&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) sensation known as [de qi sensation](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=467835&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) occurs, in which the patient may feel heaviness, numbness, or tingling during an acupuncture treatment. Length and frequency of treatment vary according to the [condition](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=651193&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) being treated. An acupuncture [treatment course](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=796498&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) for cancer symptoms or treatment of side effects is often given as multiple sessions per week.[[23](#cit/section_2.23)] Needles are typically left in place for 15 to 30 minutes after insertion, and their effects may be augmented with manual or electrical stimulation and/or heat (e.g., [moxibustion](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=449724&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) or heat lamps).

Classical techniques of acupuncture include the following:

- Needling.
- Moxibustion.
- [Cupping](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=449725&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov).

Acupressure, using fingers or mechanical [devices](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=635288&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) to apply pressure on acupuncture points is based on the same principles as acupuncture. Moxibustion is a method in which an herb (*Artemisia vulgaris*) is burned above the skin or on an acupuncture point for the purpose of warming it to alleviate symptoms. Cupping promotes [blood](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=270735&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) [circulation](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=476484&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) and stimulates acupuncture points by creating a vacuum or negative pressure on the surface of the skin.[[22](#cit/section_2.22)] During the past several decades, various new auxiliary devices have been developed. Acupuncture devices such as [electroacupuncture](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=269415&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) (EA) machines and heat lamps are commonly used to enhance the effects of acupuncture.

In addition to classical acupuncture techniques, other techniques have been developed and are sometimes used in cancer management. These include [trigger point acupuncture](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=449745&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), [laser acupuncture](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=467844&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), [acupuncture point injection](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=467825&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), and techniques focusing on particular regions of the body such as:

- [Auricular](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=449817&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) acupuncture.
- Scalp acupuncture.
- Face acupuncture.
- Hand acupuncture.
- Nose acupuncture.
- Foot acupuncture.
- Wrist-ankle acupuncture.

Of these, auricular acupuncture is the most commonly used.

In clinical practice, most acupuncturists in the United States use the traditional theories and principles of Chinese medicine. A 2017 survey of 472 licensed acupuncturists in the San Francisco Bay area reported that 77% were caring for patients with cancer, and 44% have training specific to the needs of patients with cancer.[[24](#cit/section_2.24)]

Although acupuncture has been practiced for millennia, it has come under rigorous scientific investigation only recently. In 1976, the [U.S. Food and Drug Administration](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=454785&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) (FDA) classified [acupuncture needles](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=449728&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) as [investigational](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44763&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) devices (class III) ([www.fda.gov](https://www.fda.gov/ "https://www.fda.gov/")), resulting in a number of [research studies](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=651211&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) on the effectiveness and safety of acupuncture.[[25](#cit/section_2.25)] In November 1994, the Office of Alternative Medicine (the predecessor of the [National Center for Complementary and Integrative Health](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=454777&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov)) at the [National Institutes of Health](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44268&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) (NIH) sponsored an NIH-FDA workshop on the status of acupuncture needle usage. Two years later, the FDA reclassified acupuncture needles as [medical devices](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=449732&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) (class II) without, however, giving specific indications for their use.[[26](#cit/section_2.26)] In 1997, NIH held a [Consensus Development Conference](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=467833&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) on [acupuncture](https://consensus.nih.gov/1997/1997acupuncture107html.htm "https://consensus.nih.gov/1997/1997acupuncture107html.htm") to evaluate its safety and [efficacy.](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=346517&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) The 12-member panel concluded that promising research results showing the efficacy of acupuncture in certain conditions have emerged and that further research is likely to uncover additional areas in which acupuncture intervention will be useful. The panel stated that “there is clear evidence that needle acupuncture treatment is effective for postoperative and chemotherapy N/V.” It also stated that there are “a number of other pain-related conditions for which acupuncture may be effective as an [adjunct therapy](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=467826&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), an acceptable alternative, or as part of a comprehensive treatment program,” and it agreed that further research is likely to uncover additional areas in which acupuncture intervention will be useful.[[25](#cit/section_2.25)]

These actions by the FDA and NIH have resulted in the establishment of several active programs of research into the mechanisms and efficacy of acupuncture, much of which is, or is potentially, relevant to cancer management. The most extensively investigated aspect of these mechanisms has been the effect of acupuncture on pain management. The NIH Consensus Panel concluded that “acupuncture can cause multiple [biological](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44510&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) responses,” local and [distal](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=256557&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), “mediated mainly by [sensory](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44121&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) [neurons](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=269444&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov)…within the [central nervous system](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46481&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov).” Acupuncture “may also [activate](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=43976&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) the [hypothalamus](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46359&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) and the [pituitary gland](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46229&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), resulting in a broad spectrum of [systemic](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45921&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) effects,” including “alterations in [peptides](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45823&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), [hormones](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45713&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) and [neurotransmitters](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=458085&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) and the regulation of blood flow.”[[25](#cit/section_2.25)] The effect of acupuncture on chronic [inflammatory](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=373080&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) pain has been studied.[[27](#cit/section_2.27),[28](#cit/section_2.28)] Evidence suggests that acupuncture operates through the [autonomic nervous system](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=446115&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) to balance the [sympathetic](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=467867&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) and [parasympathetic](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=467855&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) systems and suggests that the [anti-inflammatory](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44187&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) effects of acupuncture are mediated by its electrophysiologic effects on neurotransmitters, [cytokines](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46130&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), and [neuropeptides](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44064&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov).[[1](#cit/section_2.1),[28](#cit/section_2.28)-[37](#cit/section_2.37)] Many studies provide evidence that [opioid](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45054&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) peptides are released during acupuncture and that acupuncture [analgesia](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=449929&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) is mediated by the [endogenous](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44026&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) opioid system.[[38](#cit/section_2.38),[39](#cit/section_2.39)]

Although the mechanism of acupuncture is not fully understood, it has been proposed that beneficial results are mediated by changes in neurohormones and cytokines. Animal research suggests that acupuncture achieves its [anesthetic](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46051&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) effect by stimulating [nerves](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=373935&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) in the muscle, which then relay the signal to the [spinal cord](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=340937&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), midbrain, and hypothalamus-pituitary system, ultimately triggering release of neurotransmitters and hormones, such as endorphins and enkephalins.[[40](#cit/section_2.40),[41](#cit/section_2.41)]

Laboratory and animal cancer studies have also explored the mechanisms of acupuncture through the activation and modulation of the [immune system](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46356&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov). Previous animal and human studies have suggested that acupuncture worked through [immunomodulation,](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=367426&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) with [significant](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=390271&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) changes in cytokines including [interleukin](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46069&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) (IL)-1, IL-6, IL-8, IL-10, and [tumor necrosis factor](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45290&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov)-alpha (TNF-alpha).[[42](#cit/section_2.42)-[46](#cit/section_2.46)] These studies were limited by small sample size and occasional conflicting results. Acupuncture has been associated with significant changes in proinflammatory cytokines including IL-1-beta, IL-6, IL-17, and TNF-alpha.[[42](#cit/section_2.42)-[47](#cit/section_2.47)] In addition, studies showed that acupuncture needle manipulation stimulated surrounding connective tissues and sensory nerves [[48](#cit/section_2.48)] and affected adenosine-mediated peripheral sensory modulation.[[49](#cit/section_2.49)]

Acupuncture treatment points are located by using standard [anatomic](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=467827&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) landmarks and [comparative anatomy](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=467831&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov). EA is the most commonly used treatment intervention; a few studies have used moxibustion.[[50](#cit/section_2.50)] These studies
show that acupuncture may boost animal [immune function](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45721&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) by enhancing NK cell and lymphocyte activity.[[50](#cit/section_2.50)-[52](#cit/section_2.52)] According to one [animal study](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=454774&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), acupuncture may be a useful adjuvant for suppressing chemotherapy-induced [emesis](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=256560&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov).[[53](#cit/section_2.53)]

Although several studies published in China examined the effect of acupuncture on the human immune system,[[8](#cit/section_2.8),[35](#cit/section_2.35),[38](#cit/section_2.38),[54](#cit/section_2.54)-[57](#cit/section_2.57)] most cancer-related human [clinical studies](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44195&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) of acupuncture evaluated its effect on patient quality of life. These investigations mainly focused on cancer symptoms or cancer treatment–related symptoms, predominantly cancer pain [[10](#cit/section_2.10),[29](#cit/section_2.29),[58](#cit/section_2.58)-[62](#cit/section_2.62)] and chemotherapy-induced N/V.[[31](#cit/section_2.31),[33](#cit/section_2.33),[63](#cit/section_2.63)-[71](#cit/section_2.71)] Studies have also evaluated the effect of acupuncture on [radiation](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45072&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov)-induced xerostomia (dry mouth), [proctitis](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=454770&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), [dysphonia](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=449747&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), weight loss, cough, [thoracodynia](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=449748&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), [hemoptysis](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=449749&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), [fever](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=450108&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), [esophageal](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46414&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) [obstruction](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44954&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), poor appetite, night sweats, hot flashes in women and men,[[72](#cit/section_2.72)] dizziness, fatigue, anxiety, and depression in cancer patients.[[8](#cit/section_2.8)-[10](#cit/section_2.10),[73](#cit/section_2.73)-[76](#cit/section_2.76)] The evidence from most of these [clinical](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44168&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) studies is inconclusive, despite their positive results; either poor research design or incompletely described [methodologic](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=467848&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) procedures limit their value.[[77](#cit/section_2.77)] There is controversy about the most appropriate control for acupuncture, which also limits the interpretability of the results of clinical trials.[[78](#cit/section_2.78)] The positive results of the studies on chemotherapy-induced N/V, which benefit from scientifically sound research designs, are the most convincing.

A 2018 [retrospective](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44120&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) [analysis](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=390238&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) of [prospectively](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44079&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) collected data of 375 cancer survivors who received acupuncture treatments at the MD Anderson Cancer Center Integrative Medicine Center outpatient clinic showed that patients experienced short- and long-term improvement in multiple symptoms including hot flashes, fatigue, numbness, tingling, and nausea.[[79](#cit/section_2.79)]

## History

The generally accepted history of [acupuncture](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46724&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov)/[moxibustion](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=449724&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) (known as zhen jiu) is part of [traditional Chinese medicine](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=449722&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) (TCM), an indigenous, coherent system of medicine that has been practiced in China for thousands of years. The history of acupuncture/moxibustion in China can be traced back archaeologically at least 4,000 years, when bian (stone needles) were in use. During the long history of recorded practice, acupuncture has been applied to many [disorders](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=407758&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov). The earliest written medical text, the ancient classic *Huang Di Nei Jing* (*Yellow Emperor's Inner Classic*, second century BC), records nine types of needles and their [therapeutic](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=43985&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) functions.

The dissemination of acupuncture and TCM to other regions dates back centuries, first to Korea and Japan and then to other Asian countries.[[1](#cit/section_3.1)] The use of acupuncture in Europe was documented in the middle of the 16th century.[[2](#cit/section_3.2)] The relatively brief history of acupuncture in the United States can be traced back about 200 years, when Dr. Franklin Bache published a report in the *North American Medical and Surgical Journal* on his use of acupuncture to treat lower back pain.[[3](#cit/section_3.3)] However, until the 1970s, when U.S.-Chinese diplomatic ties were resumed, the practice of acupuncture in this country was mainly limited to Chinatowns.[[4](#cit/section_3.4)]

For centuries, Chinese acupuncturists treated [cancer](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45333&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) symptomatically. Ancient literature and acupuncture textbooks classify cancer as a Zhi [syndrome](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45090&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) or [blood stasis](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=454775&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) condition and document acupuncture treatment principles and methods.[[5](#cit/section_3.5)-[7](#cit/section_3.7)] Since the development of modern [conventional medicine](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=449752&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), acupuncture has primarily been used clinically as an adjunct to conventional cancer treatment.

## Laboratory/Animal/Preclinical Studies

At least seven [animal studies](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=454774&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) investigating the effects of [acupuncture](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46724&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) in [cancer](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45333&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) or cancer-related [conditions](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=651193&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) have been reported in the scientific literature.[[1](#cit/section_4.1)-[5](#cit/section_4.5)] Two of the studies were conducted in China, one of which was published in Chinese with an English abstract. One study was conducted in Japan, one in Sweden, and one in the United States. Four of the studies were *[ex vivo](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=467838&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov)* laboratory investigations using [blood](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=270735&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) or [tissue](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46683&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) samples;[[1](#cit/section_4.1)-[3](#cit/section_4.3),[5](#cit/section_4.5)] the remaining study was an animal behavioral study testing the effect of acupuncture on [chemotherapy](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45214&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov)-induced [nausea](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=390302&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) and [vomiting.](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=390324&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) [[4](#cit/section_4.4)]

The four *ex vivo* studies suggested that acupuncture is useful in anticancer [therapy](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44737&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) either by actively stimulating [immune activity](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45722&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) or by preventing chemotherapy suppression of immune activity.[[1](#cit/section_4.1)-[3](#cit/section_4.3),[5](#cit/section_4.5)]

In a study involving normal rats, [electroacupuncture](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=269415&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) (EA) (1 Hz, 5–20 V, 1-millisecond pulse width, 2 hours) applied at the point Zu-Sanli (S36) for 2 hours daily on 3 consecutive days enhanced the [cytotoxicity](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44020&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) of [splenic](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=467863&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) [natural killer (NK) cells](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44062&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) compared with a stimulation of a nonacupuncture control point in the [abdominal](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46684&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) muscle.[[3](#cit/section_4.3)]

Another study found that NK [cell](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46476&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) activity and T-[lymphocyte](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45765&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) [transformation](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46628&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) rate were increased in a [mouse model](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=561606&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) of [transplanted](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46631&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) [mammary](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44560&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) cancer compared with a control (*P* < .05) after eight sessions of acupuncture and [moxibustion](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=449724&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov).[[2](#cit/section_4.2)]

A study involving [tumor](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46634&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov)-bearing mice [(sarcoma](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45562&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) S180) using moxibustion to warm the [acupuncture point](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=449727&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) Guanyuan (CV4) once a day for 10 days found [significantly](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=390271&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) increased production of [erythrocytes](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46425&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), compared with a nontreatment control.[[1](#cit/section_4.1)]

The fourth *ex vivo* study used a rat model to investigate the effect of EA on [nerve growth factor](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=449760&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) (NGF), which is associated with [polycystic ovary syndrome](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=454780&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) (PCOS). Women with PCOS have an increased risk of [endometrial cancer](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=444987&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) and other diseases. Repeated EA treatments (12 treatments administered over 30 days) in PCO rats significantly lowered the concentrations of NGF in the [ovaries,](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46687&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) compared with untreated PCO rats.[[5](#cit/section_4.5)]

A study of [cyclophosphamide](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45253&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov)-induced [emesis](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=256560&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) in a ferret behavioral model used acupuncture as an [adjunct therapy](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=467826&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) in treating the [emetic](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=467221&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) [side effects](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46580&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) of chemotherapy. EA at 100 Hz, 1.5 V, for 10 minutes in combination with subeffective [doses](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44664&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) of [antiemetics](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46084&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) such as [ondansetron](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=616146&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) (0.04 mg/kg), [droperidol](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=467837&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) (0.25 mg/kg), and [metoclopramide](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45225&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) (2.24 mg/kg) significantly reduced the total number of emetic episodes by 52%, 36%, and 73%, respectively (*P* < .01), in this ferret model.[[4](#cit/section_4.4)]

A rat model has been established by [injecting](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44678&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) AT-3.1 [prostate cancer](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=445079&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) cells into the [tibia](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=467871&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) of the adult male Copenhagen rat, which closely mimics prostate cancer-induced [bone cancer](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=434562&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) pain.[[6](#cit/section_4.6)] The cancer-caused pain was treated with 10 Hz EA for 30 minutes a day at acupuncture point [gallbladder](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46395&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) 30 (GB30) from days 14 to 18 after cancer-cell injection. For sham control, EA needles were inserted into GB30 without stimulation. Thermal [hyperalgesia](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=791321&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), a decrease in paw withdrawal latency to a noxious thermal stimulus, and mechanical hyperalgesia, a decrease in paw withdrawal pressure threshold, were measured at [baseline](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=467830&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) and 20 minutes after EA. EA significantly [attenuated](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44580&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) the hyperalgesia compared with sham control. Moreover, the EA inhibited up-regulation of preprodynorphin [mRNA](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=662001&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) and dynorphin as well as [interleukin](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46069&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov)-1beta (IL-1beta) and its mRNA compared with sham control. [Intrathecal](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45740&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) injection of antiserum against dynorphin A (1–17) and IL-1 [receptor](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44958&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) [antagonist](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=350250&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) significantly inhibited the cancer-induced hyperalgesia. These data suggests that EA alleviates bone cancer pain at least in part by suppressing spinal dynorphin and IL-1beta expression.[[7](#cit/section_4.7),[8](#cit/section_4.8)]

Another [cutaneous](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46112&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) cancer pain model has been established by injecting B16-BL6 [melanoma](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45135&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) cells into the plantar region of one hind paw of C57BL/6 mice. A single EA treatment showed significant [analgesia](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=449929&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) on day 8 but not on day 20. EA treatments once every other day starting on day 8 showed analgesia at day 20, but EA starting on day 16 did not. The results indicate
that EA exerts antihyperalgesic effects on early [stage](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45885&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) but not on late stage cutaneous cancer pain.[[9](#cit/section_4.9)] These animal studies support the [clinical](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44168&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) use of EA in the treatment of cancer pain.

The findings of these studies suggest that acupuncture may be effective in treating cancer-related [symptoms](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45022&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) and cancer treatment–related [disorders](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=407758&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) and that acupuncture may be able to [activate](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=43976&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) [immune functions](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45721&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) [[1](#cit/section_4.1)-[3](#cit/section_4.3)] and regulate the [autonomic nervous system](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=446115&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov).[[4](#cit/section_4.4),[5](#cit/section_4.5)] Only one study reported a decrease in [tumor volume](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=467873&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) in animals treated with acupuncture compared with [control animals](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44585&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov); however, the scientific value of this report is limited because of insufficient information about the research [methodology.](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=467848&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) [[2](#cit/section_4.2)]

## Human/Clinical Studies

### Effect of Acupuncture on Immune Function

There has been limited research, mostly performed in China, evaluating the effect of [acupuncture](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46724&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) on [immune system](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46356&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) function in [cancer](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45333&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) patients, suggesting that acupuncture improves [immune function](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45721&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov).[[1](#cit/section_5.1)-[6](#cit/section_5.6)]

### Effect of Acupuncture on Cancer Pain

[Clinical studies](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44195&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) and reviews of acupuncture as a treatment for cancer-related pain have been reported in the English language (see [Table 1](/about-cancer/treatment/cam/hp/acupuncture-pdq#_57)).[[7](#cit/section_5.7)-[10](#cit/section_5.10)] Two studies were [randomized](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45858&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) [controlled trials](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44014&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) (RCTs), with one study conducted in China and one in France.[[7](#cit/section_5.7),[8](#cit/section_5.8)] Four studies were [case series](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44006&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), with one each from England, France, Hong Kong, and the United States.[[11](#cit/section_5.11)-[14](#cit/section_5.14)]

One randomized trial compared classical Chinese acupuncture, [acupuncture point injection](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=467825&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) with [freeze-dried](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44150&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) human [transfer factor](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=449883&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), and conventional [analgesic](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45590&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) treatment in patients with [gastric cancer](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=454513&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) pain.[[7](#cit/section_5.7)] The investigators reported an equivalent analgesic effect among the three groups observed after 2 months of treatment; however, the conventionally treated group experienced significantly superior [analgesia](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=449929&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) compared with both acupuncture treatment groups during the first 10 days of treatment. The researchers reported that the patients in both acupuncture treatment groups also experienced improved [quality of life](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45417&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) (QOL) and a decrease in the [side effects](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46580&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) of [chemotherapy](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45214&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), in addition to analgesia.

A randomized, blinded, controlled trial (N = 90) reported that cancer pain intensity was significantly decreased (by 36%) in an [auricular acupuncture](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=793175&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) treatment group, in comparison with [control groups](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44149&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) (by 2%, acupuncture at [placebo](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46688&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) points or auricular seeds placed at placebo points) after 2 months of treatment (*P* < .001).[[8](#cit/section_5.8)]

Although most of these studies were positive and demonstrated the effectiveness of acupuncture in cancer pain control, the findings have limited significance because of [methodologic](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=467848&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) weaknesses such as small sample sizes, an absence of patient blinding to treatment in most cases, varying acupuncture treatment [regimens](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45864&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), a lack of standard [outcome](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=467853&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) measurements, and an absence of adequate [randomization](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=322881&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov). A 2015 Cochrane systematic review of five RCTs reported benefits of acupuncture in reducing [pancreatic cancer](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44521&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) pain, pain from [late-stage cancer](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=561600&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), and [chronic](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45641&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) cancer-related neuropathic pain; the study found no difference between real and sham [electroacupuncture](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=269415&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) (EA) for [ovarian cancer](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=445074&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) pain.[[15](#cit/section_5.15)] However, because of small sample sizes and a high risk of [bias](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44646&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), the authors concluded, “there is insufficient evidence to judge whether acupuncture is effective in treating cancer pain in adults.”[[15](#cit/section_5.15)]

In addition, a 2016 systematic review and [meta-analysis](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=691484&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) of 1,639 participants with cancer-related pain in 20 RCTs with a high risk of [bias](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44646&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) showed that acupuncture alone was not superior to conventional [drug therapy](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=651195&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), although acupuncture plus drug therapy appeared to be superior to drug therapy alone.[[16](#cit/section_5.16)] However, this study was limited by the poor quality of [combination therapy](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=688810&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) trials. Further investigations into the
effects of acupuncture on cancer pain using rigorous scientific [methodology](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=467848&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) are warranted.

Most acupuncture cancer pain [clinical trials](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45961&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) use conventional body acupuncture. A 2020 clinical trial studied the effect of wrist-ankle acupuncture in combination with auricular acupuncture to treat cancer pain and showed that the combination therapy is effective in reducing pain and analgesic medication usage.[[17](#cit/section_5.17)] The study is limited by small sample size, lack of placebo control, short [follow-up](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44671&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), lack of description of treatment toxicities (e.g., [infection](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45364&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) or bleeding), and limited outcome measurement with using a numeric rating scale only.

### Effect of Acupuncture on Cancer Treatment–Related Side Effects

#### Pain

##### Acupuncture for postsurgical pain

Five RCTs published in English have addressed the use of acupuncture for pain related to cancer treatment, mostly postsurgical pain (see [Table 2](/about-cancer/treatment/cam/hp/acupuncture-pdq#_262)). One RCT of 106 cancer patients who experienced postthoracotomy pain showed no statistical difference in the real acupuncture (RA) group compared with the sham acupuncture (SA) group in patients' pain scores measured by the [Brief Pain Inventory](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45627&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) at the 30-, 60-, and 90-day follow-up.[[18](#cit/section_5.18)] The [efficacy](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=346517&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) of the unique [intradermal](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44602&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) needles used in this study was questionable.

One RCT (N = 93) compared acupuncture with [massage therapy](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=454761&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) and usual care in controlling postoperative pain, [nausea](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=390302&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), [vomiting](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=390324&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), and depressive moods.[[19](#cit/section_5.19)] This study showed that postoperative acupuncture and massage in addition to usual care significantly improved pain control when compared with usual care alone.

Another study showed that in cancer patients with [chronic pain](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44654&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) or [dysfunction](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=390268&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) as a result of [neck dissection](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46281&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), four weekly acupuncture treatments significantly reduced pain and improved function compared with [standard care](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44930&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) alone.[[20](#cit/section_5.20)] Additionally, a study of 80 patients with [breast cancer](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=444971&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) showed that when compared with usual care alone, acupuncture significantly improved postoperative pain and range of movement.[[21](#cit/section_5.21)] However, with no [sham therapy](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45078&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) group in these two studies, it is difficult to determine how much of the improvement is because of the placebo effect, and whether RA needles and professionally trained acupuncturists and massage therapists are required in the [intervention](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=454757&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov).

[Acupressure](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46723&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) has been shown to be efficacious in reducing procedural pain. Two RCTs showed that acupressure at LI4 and HT7 significantly reduced patients’ pain and [anxiety](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=430405&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov).[[22](#cit/section_5.22),[23](#cit/section_5.23)]

##### Acupuncture for aromatase inhibitor-associated musculoskeletal symptoms

A 2012 meta-analysis of 29 trials with 17,922 patients found that RA is more beneficial than both SA and no acupuncture in the treatment of chronic pain, with a modest effect size of 0.23 (95% confidence interval [CI], 0.13–0.33).[[24](#cit/section_5.24)] However, none of the patients in these trials had pain due to cancer or cancer therapies. Recently published meta-analyses have examined outcomes from RCTs evaluating the effects of acupuncture on [aromatase inhibitor](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44232&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov)-associated [musculoskeletal](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44938&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) symptoms (AIMSS) in breast cancer [survivors](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=450125&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) with a history of [stage](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45885&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) I, II, or III [nonmetastatic](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45805&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) [hormone receptor–positive](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=788029&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) breast cancer currently taking an aromatase inhibitor.

Three meta-analyses [[25](#cit/section_5.25)-[27](#cit/section_5.27)] identified five studies [[28](#cit/section_5.28)-[32](#cit/section_5.32)] that randomly assigned participants to receive SA or RA. Four of the studies were conducted in the United States and one was conducted in Australia. Two studies used EA [[30](#cit/section_5.30),[31](#cit/section_5.31)] and three studies used manual acupuncture (MA).[[28](#cit/section_5.28),[29](#cit/section_5.29),[32](#cit/section_5.32)] Three of these studies were sham-controlled; one randomly assigned patients to receive either acupuncture followed by [observation](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45981&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) or vice versa, and one was a three-arm study where participants were randomly assigned to SA, EA, or wait-list control (WLC). All studies had relatively small sample sizes ranging from 19 to 67.

All studies included in the meta-analyses [[25](#cit/section_5.25)-[27](#cit/section_5.27)] classified changes in measures of [joint](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=458080&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) pain and stiffness as primary outcomes. Participant symptom improvement was assessed by using self-reported measures for pain, and pain interference and stiffness at [baseline](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=467830&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) and at specified intervals during and after the intervention. Results from these meta-analyses were not definitive and suggested further research needs to be conducted in this area.

Further extending the literature supporting the efficacy of acupuncture in treating AIMSS, an RCT was conducted using 226 participants (SWOG-S1200 [[NCT01535066](/clinicaltrials/NCT01535066)]) randomly assigned to three groups (MA, SA, and WLC) and found improvements in joint pain after treatment when compared with SA and WLC.[[32](#cit/section_5.32)] These findings are of uncertain clinical significance because the magnitude of difference in the primary outcome measure between groups (MA vs. SA) was less than the amount that was predetermined as being clinically meaningful by the researchers. However, these findings are consistent with results from existing observational studies showing the safety and feasibility of acupuncture in treating pain.

##### Acupuncture for musculoskeletal pain in cancer survivors

In the 2021 Personalized Electroacupuncture versus Auricular Acupuncture Comparativeness Effectiveness (PEACE) trial, 360 cancer survivors with moderate to severe musculoskeletal pain (without evidence of disease) for at least 3 months were randomly assigned in a 2:2:1 ratio to receive electroacupuncture (EA), auricular acupuncture (AA), or usual care. Both EA and AA significantly reduced average pain severity on a 0 to 10–point pain scale (EA, 1.9; 95% CI, 1.4–2.4; AA, 1.6; 95% CI, 1.0–2.1) when compared with usual care. Because of ear pain, 10.5% of patients dropped out the AA group.[[33](#cit/section_5.33)]

#### Nausea and vomiting

##### Chemotherapy-induced nausea and vomiting

Of all the investigated effects of acupuncture on cancer-related or chemotherapy-related symptoms and [disorders](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=407758&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), the positive effect of acupuncture on chemotherapy-induced nausea and vomiting (N/V) is the most convincing, as demonstrated by the consistency of the results of a variety of clinical study types, including RCTs, [nonrandomized trials](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44160&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), [prospective](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44079&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) [consecutive case series](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=285747&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), and [retrospective](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44120&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) studies (see [Table 4](/about-cancer/treatment/cam/hp/acupuncture-pdq#_357)).
Consistent with the findings from clinical studies of acupuncture on N/V due to other causes (i.e., postoperative N/V and morning sickness), these studies showed acupuncture to be effective in the treatment of chemotherapy-induced N/V (CINV).

A 2013 systematic review of literature on acupuncture in cancer care screened 2,151 publications and identified 41 RCTs studying the effect of using acupuncture to treat eight cancer treatment–related symptoms (pain, nausea, [hot flashes](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=256567&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), [fatigue](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=321374&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), radiation-induced [xerostomia](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44537&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), prolonged postoperative [ileus](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=558995&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), anxiety/mood disorders, and sleep disturbance). The review concluded that acupuncture is an appropriate adjunctive treatment for CINV, but additional studies are needed because most RCTs had unclear bias or a high risk of bias.[[34](#cit/section_5.34)]

In 2005, a comprehensive meta-analysis of 11 RCTs (N = 1,247) evaluating the effect of acupuncture-point stimulation in controlling CINV showed that acupuncture-point stimulation significantly reduced the proportion of [acute](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45585&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) vomiting ([relative risk](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=618613&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), 0.82; 95% CI, 0.69–0.99, *P* = .04),[[35](#cit/section_5.35)] although the meta-analysis did not show that acupuncture reduced the [mean](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=285973&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) number of acute [emetic](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=467221&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) episodes or acute or delayed nausea severity compared with control.

The trials in the meta-analysis were published between 1987 and 2003, and the sample sizes ranged from ten patients in the smallest trial [[36](#cit/section_5.36)] to 747 patients in the largest trial.[[37](#cit/section_5.37)] Among the ten trials that reported a chemotherapy regimen, all patients received moderate to high [emetogenic](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=454776&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) chemotherapy. Eight of the trials used ondansetron, a [5-HT3-receptor antagonist](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=467824&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), as the [antiemetic](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46084&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) regimen. The other three trials used [methotrexate](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45393&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) alone, methotrexate with [prednisone](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45492&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), or methotrexate with dopaminergic antagonists as the antiemetic regimen.[[35](#cit/section_5.35)] None of the antiemetic regimens contained [aprepitant](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=468778&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) because the trials all predated this drug.

A meta-analysis of acupuncture in N/V is the most comprehensive summary of clinical research on the role of acupuncture-point stimulation in controlling CINV. It found that acupuncture-point stimulation decreases the proportion of patients who experience acute chemotherapy-induced vomiting and concurred with the previous systemic review and meta-analysis.[[38](#cit/section_5.38),[39](#cit/section_5.39)] It suggested that acupressure may relieve chemotherapy-induced nausea, even though the studies were limited by lack of an effective control arm to rule out the placebo effect. It also suggested differences among acupuncture-point stimulation modalities, with invasive-point stimulation to be more effective than [noninvasive](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=455364&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov)-point stimulation in reducing acute CINV.[[35](#cit/section_5.35)] It has since been cited multiple times by review articles and [oncology](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45434&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) practice guidelines.[[40](#cit/section_5.40)-[42](#cit/section_5.42)]

The [National Institutes of Health](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44268&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) Consensus Development Conference held in 1997 reviewed studies that evaluated the safety and efficacy of acupuncture in treating postoperative- and CINV.[[43](#cit/section_5.43)] Studies discussed at the conference reported significantly less N/V compared with the control group.[[36](#cit/section_5.36),[44](#cit/section_5.44)-[47](#cit/section_5.47)] The panel stated that “there is clear evidence that needle acupuncture treatment is effective for postoperative and chemotherapy N/V.”[[43](#cit/section_5.43)]

The acupuncture point [specificity](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=322884&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) is worth mentioning because most of the earlier acupuncture CINV trials used the PC6 acupuncture point and showed positive results. A well-designed, randomized, [placebo-controlled](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44840&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) trial published in 2014 showed that K1 [acupoint](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=454748&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) [acustimulation](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46725&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) combined with antiemetics did not prevent [cisplatin](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45230&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov)-induced or [oxaliplatin](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45465&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov)-induced nausea in 103 [liver cancer](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44242&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) patients who underwent a [transarterial chemoembolization](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=663329&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) (TACE) procedure.[[48](#cit/section_5.48)] A single-blind, randomized, controlled trial in 2017 showed that transcutaneous electrical stimulation at P6, LI4, and ST36 acupoints did not significantly alleviate CINV associated with TACE, when compared with placebo in patients with liver cancer.[[49](#cit/section_5.49)] EA at P6, LI4, and ST36 points did, however, reduce [anorexia](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44103&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) scores more than SA.

A 2016 RCT showed that among 48 breast cancer patients receiving chemotherapy, patients randomly assigned to the auricular acupressure group (ear seed placed on point zero, [stomach,](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46604&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) brainstem, shenmen, and cardia) had significantly less intense and less frequent N/V in acute and delayed phases compared with the control group that had no auricular acupressure.[[50](#cit/section_5.50)] This study is limited by its small sample size and lack of placebo control. However, it describes an additional noninvasive method to control CINV that may deserve further investigation.

Researchers have attempted to augment the impact of acupressure on the antiemetic effects using certain methods, but they were not found to be effective.[[51](#cit/section_5.51)]

A 2017 RCT that compared acupuncture with SA in 60 [multiple myeloma](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45793&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) patients undergoing [bone marrow transplantation](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45263&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) (BMT) showed that even though acupuncture did not significantly improve overall symptoms during and 15 days after BMT compared with SA, it was significantly more efficacious in reducing nausea, lack of [appetite](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=454699&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), and drowsiness at 15 days after BMT.[[52](#cit/section_5.52)] Patients who received SA were more likely to increase pain medication usage post-BMT.

A 2020 RCT examined the effect of real acupuncture versus sham acupuncture in reducing CINV among 134 patients with [advanced cancer](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=478743&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov). Patients received treatment twice a day on the first day of chemotherapy and once a day for the next 4 days. No significant difference was found between the two groups.[[53](#cit/section_5.53)] Low CINV scores throughout the trial is a limitation of the study. The ceiling effect left little room for improvement in CINV scores.

##### Radiation-induced nausea and vomiting

Acupuncture has also been used to relieve radiation-induced N/V. In one randomized study, patients who were randomly assigned to receive either [verum](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=729833&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) or SA experienced fewer episodes of N/V than did those who received standard care.[[54](#cit/section_5.54)]

#### Vasomotor symptoms

Some studies have reported that acupuncture may be effective in reducing [vasomotor](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=256578&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) symptoms among [postmenopausal](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45269&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) women with breast cancer and [prostate cancer](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=445079&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) patients receiving [androgen-deprivation therapy](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=559086&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov).[[61](#cit/section_5.61)-[67](#cit/section_5.67)]

Six RCTs have studied the role of acupuncture in reducing hot flashes in breast cancer survivors.

In 2007, one study reported results from a randomized, sham-controlled trial on the effect of acupuncture in treating breast cancer survivors who experienced three or more hot flashes per day.[[61](#cit/section_5.61)] The investigators reported that the mean number of hot flashes per day at week 6 changed from 8.7 to 6.2 in the RA arm, and from 10.0 to 7.6 in the SA arm. However, the difference between the RA group and the SA group was not statistically significant (*P* = .3). When patients in the SA group crossed over to receive RA, their hot flash frequency further reduced from 7.6 to 5.8. The reduction in hot flashes in all patients persisted during the 6 months of follow-up (RA arm, 6.1 per day; SA arm, 6.8 per day). On the basis of fewer hot flashes in both groups, the authors concluded that acupuncture reduced hot-flash frequency, although the difference between the RA and SA groups was not statistically significant.[[61](#cit/section_5.61)]

In 2009, one study reported another randomized SA-controlled trial on the effect of acupuncture in treating women with breast cancer who suffered from hot flashes after receiving [tamoxifen](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=509341&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) for at least 3 months.[[68](#cit/section_5.68)] Fifty-nine women were randomly assigned to either 15 sessions (5 weeks biweekly followed by 5 weeks weekly) of RA or SA. The authors reported that at the end of the treatment period, the mean number of daytime hot flashes was reduced significantly from 9.5 to 4.7 (*P* = .001) in the RA group and from 12.3 to 11.7 (*P* = .382) in the SA group. At 12 weeks follow-up, further reduction was observed in the RA group (from 4.7 to 3.2) but not in the SA group (from 11.7 to 12.1). The difference between the RA group and SA group was statistically significant (*P* < .001). The authors concluded that acupuncture provided effective relief from hot flashes in women with breast cancer who suffered from hot flashes while taking tamoxifen.[[68](#cit/section_5.68)] The evidence generated from these two trials suggests that acupuncture effectively decreases hot-flash frequency, although it is not clear whether it is superior to SA.

Another clinical trial compared the effects of EA with [hormonal therapy](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46367&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) in breast cancer survivors with vasomotor symptoms; in 19 of 27 women who completed 12 weeks of EA treatment, the number of hot flashes was significantly reduced from 9.6 per day to 4.3 per day. The improvement persisted at the 12-month follow up.[[69](#cit/section_5.69)] In the hormonal treatment group, the [median](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44941&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) number of hot flashes dropped from 6.6 at baseline to 0 at week 12. Although hot flashes decreased less in the EA group than in the hormonal treatment group, health-related QOL improved at least to the same extent. It suggests that EA could be further evaluated as treatment for women with breast cancer and climacteric complaints, particularly since hormonal treatment is no longer recommended for breast cancer survivors.[[69](#cit/section_5.69)]

In 2010, another RCT compared the effect of acupuncture with [venlafaxine](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46117&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) in treating vasomotor symptoms in breast cancer patients suffering from more than 13 hot flashes per week.[[63](#cit/section_5.63)] Changes in hot flash frequency from baseline and at 3-, 6-, 9- and 12-month follow-up were used as the primary outcome. Fifty patients were randomly assigned to 12 weeks (biweekly for 4 weeks, followed by weekly for 8 weeks) of acupuncture versus daily venlafaxine (37.5 [mg](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44213&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) for 1 week, then 75 mg for 11 weeks). The investigators observed a significant reduction in hot flash frequency and severity in both groups. In addition, 2 weeks after treatments were stopped, patients randomly assigned to venlafaxine reported increased hot-flash frequency, whereas the acupuncture group remained at a low level of hot flashes. There was no significant difference between the acupuncture arm and the venlafaxine arm. There were 18 reported adverse events (i.e., nausea, dizziness, headache) in the venlafaxine arm and none in the acupuncture arm. The authors concluded that acupuncture appears to be as effective as venlafaxine and is a safe and durable treatment option for breast cancer patients experiencing vasomotor symptoms.[[63](#cit/section_5.63)]

In 2013, a study reported the results of a three-arm RCT (N = 94) comparing RA (N = 31) with SA (N = 29) and usual care alone (N = 34) in reducing hot flashes in breast cancer survivors. In the acupuncture group, 16 (52%) patients experienced a significant reduction in hot flashes compared with 7 (24%) in the SA group (*P* < .05). There was also a statistically positive effect on sleep when RA was compared with SA. Importantly, the researchers measured the [plasma](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45839&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) [estradiol](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=305990&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) level and determined that there was not a correlation between symptoms improvement and an increase in estradiol level.[[70](#cit/section_5.70)]

In 2014, a study reported the results of a two-arm RCT (N = 47) on the effect of acupuncture in reducing AIMSS and hot flashes as one of the secondary end points. When compared with baseline, acupuncture significantly improved hot-flash severity, frequency, and function. SA significantly improved the Hot Flash Related Daily Interference Scale only. However, there was no significant difference between the two groups.[[71](#cit/section_5.71)]

These trials once again confirmed that acupuncture is safe. They showed that acupuncture reduced hot flashes significantly when compared with baseline, although the benefit of RA versus SA was not clear.

A 2015 systematic review of acupuncture to control hot flashes in cancer patients showed that in all eight studies included in the review, acupuncture resulted in significant improvement from the baseline, and three studies showed RA was significantly better than SA in different aspects of hot flashes. However, none of the studies were rated with a low risk of bias.[[72](#cit/section_5.72)] A 2016 meta-analysis of 12 RCTs published before April 2015 on the efficacy of acupuncture or EA on breast cancer survivors suffering from hot flashes failed to show a favorable effect of acupuncture on reducing the frequency of hot flashes when compared with control groups such as SA, [hormone therapy](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45110&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), antidepressants, or relaxation intervention.[[73](#cit/section_5.73)] The authors explained that this finding may be due to heterogeneity of the studies, small sample size of the studies, and the underlying methodological issues with some studies. Another 2016 systematic review of 12 RCTs (including eight in the aforementioned meta-analysis) found that all RCTs showed the effect of acupuncture when compared with no acupuncture. However, only two of the six RCTs comparing RA with SA showed significant benefit of acupuncture over placebo. The other studies showed that acupuncture was no better than hormone therapy, venlafaxine, or relaxation controls.[[74](#cit/section_5.74)] Consequently, there is insufficient evidence supporting or refuting using acupuncture to treat hot flashes. Further studies are needed.

A study published in 2015 that used EA in the treatment of hot flashes randomly assigned 120 breast cancer survivors who were suffering from hot flashes at least twice daily to one of four of the following arms: EA, SA, [gabapentin](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45039&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) (GP), and placebo pills (PP) for 8 weeks.[[75](#cit/section_5.75)] Unlike other acupuncture efficacy trials, however, the primary end point was the change in hot flash composite scores (HFCS) between SA and PP at week 8, with secondary end points including posttreatment comparisons of all groups at different time points and examination of treatment durability at week 24. It showed that SA produced significantly greater reductions in HFCS than did PP by week 8, indicating a greater placebo effect with SA. Although all arms experienced HFCS reductions, SA produced significantly better results than did both GP and PP interventions (EA, −7.4; SA, −5.9; GP, −5.2; and PP, −3.4), compared with baseline scores. In addition, SA had a smaller [nocebo effect](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=792402&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) than PP, as evidenced by the significantly higher percentage of reported adverse events for PP (20.0%) compared with SA (3.1%). Another intriguing finding is the more-durable effect in HFCS reductions produced at week 24 (16 weeks posttreatment) with both EA and SA treatments (EA, −8.5; SA, −6.1) than with GP (GP, −2.8), suggesting that both types of acupuncture may elicit underlying [physiologic](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45837&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) changes not induced by pharmacologic intervention. On the other hand, the effect size of EA compared with SA was small at week 8 (Cohen’s d, 0.21) but got bigger at week 24 (Cohen’s d, 0.31), suggesting EA may produce additional or longer-lasting physiological effects than would SA.[[75](#cit/section_5.75)]

A 2016 pragmatic RCT (N = 190) that compared individualized acupuncture plus enhanced self-care (as described in an information booklet provided to all patients) with enhanced self-care alone showed that the combination therapy is superior to self-care alone in reducing hot flash scores at the end of treatment, at the 3-month follow-up visit, and at the 6-month follow-up visit.[[76](#cit/section_5.76)][[Level of evidence: 1iiC](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=335127&version=HealthProfessional&language=English)] In addition, this highly impactful study also showed that acupuncture significantly improved patients’ QOL without any serious [adverse effects](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44922&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov).[[76](#cit/section_5.76)] It used the standard [traditional Chinese medicine](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=449722&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) acupuncture approach, which first identified [menopausal](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46296&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) [syndromes](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45090&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) according to Maciocia’s recommendations and consequently chose individualized acupoints in addition to three common acupoints: SP6, LI11, and CV4.[[76](#cit/section_5.76)] This study provides solid evidence to support the use of acupuncture to reduce hot flashes and improve breast cancer survivors’ QOL. However, further research is needed to understand the mechanisms of how acupuncture may have reduced hot flashes.

#### Cancer-related fatigue

Fatigue is a common symptom in patients with cancer and a frequent side effect of chemotherapy and [radiation therapy](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44971&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov). No effective treatment exists. Several RCTs have been conducted to study the effect of acupuncture in reducing cancer-related fatigue (see [Table 6](/about-cancer/treatment/cam/hp/acupuncture-pdq#_263)).

One pilot RCT enrolled 47 cancer patients experiencing moderate to severe cancer fatigue and randomly assigned them to one of three groups: acupuncture (N = 15), acupressure (N = 16), or SA (N = 16). Patients in the acupuncture group received six 20-minute acupuncture sessions during a 2-week period; patients in the two acupressure groups were taught to massage RA versus SA points daily for 2 weeks.[[77](#cit/section_5.77)] The Multidimensional Fatigue Inventory was used to assess their responses at baseline, and at week 2 and week 4 follow-up. At the end of week 2, general fatigue, physical fatigue, activity, and motivation significantly improved in the acupuncture and acupressure groups when compared with baseline. At the end of week 2, fatigue level improved by 36% in the acupuncture group, 19% in the acupressure group and 0.6% in the control group. Moreover, the improvement was maintained at the week 4 follow-up. Acupuncture was found to be a more effective method than acupressure or SA. The authors concluded that acupuncture showed a greater potential for managing cancer-related fatigue; further testing in a multicenter RCT with larger sample sizes is warranted.[[77](#cit/section_5.77)] A small randomized study of 28 patients with [non-small cell lung cancer](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45323&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) reported decreased fatigue and improved QOL with RA compared with SA. The effect was sustained during the 6-week observation period.[[78](#cit/section_5.78)]

A follow-up RCT (N = 302) by the same group of investigators was published in 2013; among the 246 [evaluable patients](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44029&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), acupuncture significantly reduced cancer-related fatigue, anxiety, and [depression,](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=430479&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) and improved QOL when compared with usual care.[[79](#cit/section_5.79)] The investigators again randomly assigned 197 patients to receive 4 weeks of therapist-delivered acupuncture (N = 65), self-acupuncture (N = 67), or no acupuncture (N = 65) to determine the effect of [maintenance therapy](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45768&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) for cancer-related fatigue, and found that there was no difference between the therapist-delivered acupuncture and self-acupuncture; there was a nonsignificant trend in improving fatigue when comparing the acupuncture groups with the no-acupuncture group (*P* = .07).[[80](#cit/section_5.80)]

Conversely, two RCTs showed no significant difference between RA and SA in reducing cancer-related fatigue (see [Table 6](/about-cancer/treatment/cam/hp/acupuncture-pdq#_263)).[[81](#cit/section_5.81),[82](#cit/section_5.82)] One study reported in 2009 that among the 27 patients receiving daily radiation therapy, both weekly RA and SA treatment improved fatigue, fatigue [distress](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=454701&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), QOL, and depression from baseline to 10 weeks, but the differences between the two interventions were not significant.[[81](#cit/section_5.81)] In 2013, another study reported the results of an RCT of RA compared with SA involving 101 patients with postchemotherapy chronic fatigue. Among the 74 evaluable patients, both groups had a one-point decrease in Brief Fatigue Inventory score; however, there was no statistically significant difference between the groups. This study was limited by the large number of patients (27) lost to follow up.[[82](#cit/section_5.82)] An Australian pilot study (N = 30) showed that when compared with controls, acupuncture significantly reduced fatigue and improved well-being in patients with breast cancer patients who had posttreatment fatigue.[[83](#cit/section_5.83)]

These results showed that acupuncture significantly improved fatigue when compared with usual care alone, although whether it is significantly better than SA will warrant further study.

A 2016 pilot RCT of 78 cancer survivors with cancer-related fatigue showed that infrared [laser acupuncture](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=467844&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) point stimulation was safe in cancer patients. Patients who received infrared laser acupuncture point stimulation on ST36, CV4, and CV6 acupoints 3 times per week for 4 weeks had less fatigue than those who received sham treatment at the end of treatment (3.01 vs. 4.40; *P* = .002), and the effect lasted to week 8.[[84](#cit/section_5.84)] In addition, a 2016 large RCT of 288 breast cancer survivors with persistent fatigue ([NCT01281904](/clinicaltrials/NCT01281904)) showed that two types of acupressure (relaxing and stimulating) significantly reduced cancer fatigue.[[85](#cit/section_5.85)] Post hoc analysis revealed that acupressure was associated with reduced anxiety, pain, and depressive symptoms when compare with usual care.[[86](#cit/section_5.86)]

The AIMSS study that was published in 2014 examined the effect of acupuncture on cancer-related fatigue as a secondary [end point](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=346519&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov).[[87](#cit/section_5.87)] When compared with WLCs, EA significantly improved fatigue, anxiety, and depression. SA did not improve fatigue or anxiety but did improve depression.[[87](#cit/section_5.87)] The investigators did not compare EA with SA directly because the study was not powered to detect a difference between EA and SA, especially for secondary end points.

#### Xerostomia

A number of clinical studies have investigated the effect of acupuncture for the treatment and prevention of xerostomia in [nasopharyngeal carcinoma](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=445072&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) and [head and neck cancer](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=257519&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) patients.

Acupuncture was associated with a decrease in the onset of symptoms and an increased [saliva](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=257526&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) flow in two randomized studies that compared acupuncture with standard care for preventing xerostomia in patients undergoing radiation therapy.[[89](#cit/section_5.89),[90](#cit/section_5.90)]

Compared with standard care, acupuncture significantly improved xerostomia symptoms in patients who experienced the condition following radiation therapy.[[20](#cit/section_5.20),[91](#cit/section_5.91)]

In 2012, one group published two studies on the effect of acupuncture for preventing xerostomia. The first was a pilot study (N = 23); when compared with SA, RA significantly reduced xerostomia questionnaire scores from week 3 through the 1-month follow-up after radiation therapy. However, they did not find significant difference in salivary flow rates between the groups.[[92](#cit/section_5.92)] The other study (N = 86) showed that when compared with standard care acupuncture significantly lowered the xerostomia questionnaire scores in weeks 3 to 6 during 6 weeks of [chemoradiation](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=306495&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) therapy. In addition, greater saliva flow was noticed in the acupuncture group than in the control group at weeks 7 and 11 and at the 6-month follow-up.[[89](#cit/section_5.89)]

Another study examined long-term effects of acupuncture on xerostomia.[[93](#cit/section_5.93)] Patients who received RA were followed for 6 months and up to 3 years. Compared with baseline, significant differences in salivary flow rates were seen in patients 6 months after acupuncture treatment. At 3 years, patients who received additional acupuncture exhibited greater saliva flow rates than patients who did not continue acupuncture treatment.

One [phase III clinical trial](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45833&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) with sites in the United States and China randomly assigned 399 patients (339 patients were included in the final analysis) to receive true acupuncture (TA), SA, or standard care control (SCC), with a [primary endpoint](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44163&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) of xerostomia questionnaire score reduction at 1 year.[[94](#cit/section_5.94)] Acupuncture was given three times per week during a 6- to 7-week course of radiation therapy at the two sites. Treatment adherence was high at more than 95%. Looking at the sample as a whole, xerostomia score reduction in the TA group was greater than in the SCC group (*P* = .001; effect size, −0.44); however, only marginally different from the SA group (*P* = .06; effect size, −0.26). Of note, a post hoc analysis revealed differences in response to acupuncture between sites, suggesting a potential influence of setting (inpatient vs. [outpatient](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44520&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov)) and cultural differences on outcomes. An additional phase III clinical trial is ongoing to evaluate the effect of acupuncture for the treatment ([NCT01141231](/clinicaltrials/NCT01141231)) of xerostomia in head and neck cancer patients. Information about ongoing clinical trials is available from the [NCI website](https://www.cancer.gov/about-cancer/treatment/clinical-trials "https://www.cancer.gov/about-cancer/treatment/clinical-trials").

#### Chemotherapy-induced peripheral neuropathy

Chemotherapy-induced [peripheral neuropathy](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44705&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) (CIPN) is a common and [dose-limiting](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44170&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) side effect of neurotoxic chemotherapy. CIPN can manifest as a variety of symptoms, including pain, [paresthesia](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44118&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), [sensory](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44121&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) loss, and muscle weakness, which can lead to poor dexterity and gait disturbance. Because there is limited effective treatment available, the debilitating symptoms of CIPN can cause delay, dose-reduction, or discontinuation of life-saving treatment. Long-term CIPN often significantly impacts patients’ functional abilities and QOL. Depending on the agent used and the length of treatment, CIPN can persist long after chemotherapy completion in up to 55% of patients.[[95](#cit/section_5.95)] The current standard of care is limited to [symptom management](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=269453&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) with narcotics, [antidepressants](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44105&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), and antileptics. However, studies suggest that analgesic regimens typically produce only modest pain relief and are commonly associated with side effects such as dizziness, [sedation](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=730363&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), dry mouth, and [constipation](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=407757&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov). Acupuncture has emerged as a promising treatment [modality](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=270868&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) to reduce CIPN symptoms.

##### Acupuncture to treat persistent CIPN from taxane or platinum-based chemotherapy

Several studies have investigated the use of acupuncture to alleviate CIPN. Preliminary evidence from two small non–placebo-[controlled studies](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44015&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) (N = 5 [[96](#cit/section_5.96)] and N = 6 [[97](#cit/section_5.97)]) has shown that manual acupuncture can improve CIPN symptoms.

A three-arm RCT (N = 90) examined the use of auricular acupuncture for the treatment of chronic neuropathic pain in cancer patients after receiving cancer treatment. The study found a significant reduction in pain at 2 months in patients who were treated with acupuncture compared with patients who received the sham treatment.[[8](#cit/section_5.8)]

A multicenter observational study of 168 cancer patients who received [taxanes](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46138&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) or other neuropathy-inducing agents (i.e., [bortezomib](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=269133&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov)) compared acupuncture with acupuncture mind-body or control. The study found improvement in CIPN and related symptoms.[[98](#cit/section_5.98)]

In more recent studies, several RCTs found further promising effects of acupuncture in treating CIPN.

- A three-arm RCT (N = 75) of [solid tumor](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45301&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) patients with moderate to severe CIPN that compared RA to SA and usual care showed that RA reduced pain significantly more than usual care and at a greater degree than SA at the end of 8 weeks of treatment, and that pain reduction persisted at week 12 follow-up.[[99](#cit/section_5.99)] The analysis of QOL end points showed that both RA and SA significantly improved QOL measured by the FACT/GoG-Ntx, Hospital Anxiety and Depression Scale, and [Insomnia](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44043&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) Severity Index, compared with usual care, but no difference between RA and SA was found.[[100](#cit/section_5.100)]
- A randomized sham-controlled trial (N = 84) of [colorectal cancer](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=444983&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) patients undergoing oxaliplatin-based chemotherapy that is currently in progress seeks to find if acupuncture can improve Functional Assessment of Cancer Therapy/Gynecologic Oncology Group-Neurotoxicity (FACT/GOG-NTX) scores as the primary outcome, and numerical rating scale (NRS) and vibration and light touch sense test scores as secondary outcomes ([NCT03582423](/clinicaltrials/NCT03582423)).[[101](#cit/section_5.101)]
- A pilot study (N = 33) of cancer patients with CIPN treated with 10 sessions of acupuncture found statistical differences in groups in physical (*P* = .03) and function (*P* = .04) domains as assessed by the European Organization for Research and Treatment Quality of Life Questionnaire (EORTC QLQ-C30) and improved neuropathy sensory symptoms as assessed by the National Cancer Institute Common Terminology Criteria for Adverse Events scale ([NCT02309164](/clinicaltrials/NCT02309164)).[[102](#cit/section_5.102)]
- A small pilot study (N = 10) of breast cancer patients with [taxane](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46138&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov)-induced CIPN found that 12 sessions of acupuncture over 4 weeks significantly reduced CIPN pain as assessed by the Neuropathic Pain Symptom Inventory (NPSI) and improved sensory outcome as assessed by the Nerve Conduction Study.[[103](#cit/section_5.103)]

##### CIPN from bortezomib or thalidomide

Studies evaluating the effects of acupuncture on bortezomib and/or [thalidomide](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45458&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov)-induced peripheral neuropathy have also shown promising results. Two studies of patients with [multiple myeloma](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45793&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) (N = 27 [[104](#cit/section_5.104)] and N = 19, EA [[105](#cit/section_5.105)]) concluded that acupuncture was safe and effective in treating CIPN after 10 weeks and 9 weeks of treatment, respectively.

##### Prevention of CIPN

In a phase IIA trial of acupuncture to prevent progression of CIPN severity from weekly [paclitaxel](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45258&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) in breast cancer patients, acupuncture prevented progression of CIPN severity in 26 of 27 patients, yielding a significantly lower progression rate compared with historical controls.[[106](#cit/section_5.106)]

In a sham-controlled trial (N = 63) of 12 weekly true or sham EA for the prevention of CIPN in breast cancer patients receiving taxane, there were no differences in pain or neuropathy between groups at week 12. At week 16, patients receiving real EA were found to have worse pain (higher BPI-SF score) compared with sham.[[107](#cit/section_5.107)] Further studies need to be conducted to evaluate the role of acupuncture in CIPN prevention.

##### Acupuncture for CIPN review

A systematic review and meta-analysis of seven databases through August 2019 examined 386 cancer patients from six randomized controlled trials of high quality, based on the modified Jadad scale.[[108](#cit/section_5.108)] The meta-analysis showed that acupuncture led to significant improvements in pain scores (-1.21; 95% confidence interval [CI], -1.61 to -0.82, *P* < .00001) and [nervous system](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=446114&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) symptoms based on FACT-NTX questionnaire scores (-2.02; 95% CI, -2.21 to -1.84, *P* < .00001). The review concluded that acupuncture can effectively relieve CIPN pain and functional limitation. Another systematic review included 203 participants from three clinical trials.[[109](#cit/section_5.109)] Two studies found acupuncture to be effective in alleviating CIPN pain and improved QOL, and the third found no benefit. The review found that study quality was variable and included a moderate overall risk of bias. The review concluded that the evidence is insufficient to recommend acupuncture for the treatment or prevention of CIPN.

##### Conclusion on the use of acupuncture to treat CIPN

Emerging clinical trials comparing acupuncture with no acupuncture or SA showed promising efficacy for the use of acupuncture to reduce CIPN symptoms. Additional research needs to be done on exploring the mechanism of acupuncture to reduce CIPN symptoms and identify the sensitive population that may benefit most from acupuncture.

#### Lymphedema

Treatment-induced [lymphedema](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45359&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) may be a lifelong concern for some breast cancer survivors. There have been a number of [case reports](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44007&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), retrospective chart reviews, and pilot studies demonstrating that acupuncture was safe and potentially effective in reducing swelling and improving symptoms in patients with upper- and [lower-extremity](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=689041&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) [edema](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45676&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov).[[114](#cit/section_5.114)-[117](#cit/section_5.117)] A 2016 qualitative study of 23 breast and head and neck cancer patients with lymphedema who completed acupuncture and [moxibustion](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=449724&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) treatments showed that patients viewed acupuncture and moxibustion as a valuable whole-person approach that helped them improve their energy and decrease pain.[[118](#cit/section_5.118)]

A 2013 single-arm clinical trial (N = 37) evaluated the safety and potential efficacy of acupuncture in treating patients with breast cancer–related lymphedema (BCRL).[[117](#cit/section_5.117)] In this trial, the investigators enrolled 37 breast cancer survivors with moderate to severe [unilateral](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44989&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) chronic BCRL; the survivors received eight sessions of standard acupuncture treatment given twice per week for 4 weeks. Four patients were not able to be evaluated because of early withdrawal. Acupuncture was deemed to be safe; no serious adverse events were reported after 255 acupuncture treatment sessions. Twelve of the 33 evaluable patients reported at least one [incidence](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46145&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) of mild bruising or minor pain/tingling in the arm, shoulder, or acupuncture site; no infections were reported, although the standard acupuncture treatment protocol involved inserting four acupuncture needles in the limb with lymphedema. This pilot study, although not an RCT, suggested a trend toward efficacy with a mean reduction in arm circumference of 0.90 [cm](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=354457&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) in patients who received acupuncture treatment; eleven patients (33%) experienced at least a 30% reduction in arm circumference. Two patients did not use any additional lymphedema treatment during the trial. Most patients (28 of the remaining 31 patients) reported making no changes in their standard regimens to treat lymphedema during treatment. The authors concluded that acupuncture for BCRL is safe and may be effective. The same research group is conducting an RCT to further determine the efficacy of acupuncture in reducing BCRL symptoms ([NCT01706081](/clinicaltrials/NCT01706081)). However, in 2014, a pilot RCT of 17 women that compared acupuncture with usual care showed that 12 acupuncture treatments on the nonlymphedematous limb during 8 weeks did not change extracellular [fluid](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44669&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) or any patient-reported lymphedema outcomes.[[119](#cit/section_5.119)] The authors concluded that acupuncture may stabilize symptoms; however, further study is needed.

A 2016 RCT of 30 patients showed that warm acupuncture (acupuncture and moxibustion) improved BCRL in 51% of patients compared with 26% of patients treated with oral diosmin in the control group.[[120](#cit/section_5.120)] No adverse reaction was reported in the acupuncture and moxibustion group. The acupuncture and moxibustion group also had significant improvement in shoulder joint range of movement and QOL compared with the control group.

A RCT of acupuncture versus usual care WLC studying 82 BCRL patients did not show a significant difference in arm circumference or bioimpedance.[[121](#cit/section_5.121)]

#### Ileus

Four RCTs have studied the effect of acupuncture in reducing the duration of postoperative ileus and have generated conflicting results. In 2010, one study reported the results of an RCT studying the effect of EA compared with usual care and found that EA did not significantly prevent prolonged postoperative ileus.[[122](#cit/section_5.122)]

In 2012, another study reported the results of a phase II RCT that compared RA with SA (N = 90) for reducing postcolectomy ileus. No significant differences were reported between RA and SA in reducing postcolectomy ileus as measured by the time that the patient first tolerated solid food and the time that the patient first passed flatus or a [bowel movement](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=651179&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov).[[123](#cit/section_5.123)]

In 2013, a third study reported the results of a three-arm RCT (N = 165) that compared EA with SA and no acupuncture in reducing duration of postoperative ileus after [laparoscopic surgery](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=637280&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) for colorectal cancer. EA significantly shortened the time to [defecation](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=651180&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) and the hospital stay compared with SA and no acupuncture.[[124](#cit/section_5.124)]

A 2017 systematic review and meta-analysis of 10 RCTs involving 776 cancer patients showed that acupuncture was associated with earlier [recovery](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=454708&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) of [bowel function](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=446980&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) (shorter time to first flatus and defecation) compared with the control (no acupuncture, or SA or other types of active treatments).[[125](#cit/section_5.125)] However, the data quality was deemed low because of the high risk of bias stemming from small sample sizes and methodological limitations.

A 2023 multicenter, sham-controlled RCT conducted in China compared EA with SA in patients who underwent laparoscopic resection for colorectal cancer (N = 249). The study reported that the median time to first defecation (primary endpoint) was significantly shorter in the EA group compared with the SA group (*P* = .003).[[126](#cit/section_5.126)] Prolonged postoperative ileus occurred in 10% of the EA group versus 20% in the SA group (*P* = .03). EA after laparoscopic colon cancer surgery was safe and effective in reducing postoperative ileus.

#### Anxiety

An RCT compared preoperative acupuncture, intraoperative acupuncture, and the combination of both with conventional care for alleviating anxiety in patients undergoing surgery.[[127](#cit/section_5.127)][[Level of evidence: 1iiC](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=335127&version=HealthProfessional&language=English)] The study included 141 patients, treated in four groups. Preoperative acupuncture alleviated anxiety in the preoperative waiting area. The combination of preoperative and intraoperative acupuncture was most effective in improving postoperative QOL. Because of the lack of a sham control to assess the placebo effect, future studies using such a control are needed to determine the usefulness of this intervention.

An RCT showed that acupressure at LI4 and HT7 reduced pain and anxiety levels in patients undergoing [bone marrow biopsy](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46506&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov).[[23](#cit/section_5.23)]

Several studies have assessed improved anxiety as a secondary endpoint. An RCT that published in 2013 examined cancer-related fatigue in 246 patients. Patients reported that acupuncture significantly reduced fatigue, anxiety, and depression, and improved QOL when compared with usual care.[[79](#cit/section_5.79)] A study that published in 2014 evaluated the effects of acupuncture treatment on joint pain related to aromatase inhibitors. When acupuncture was compared with WLCs, EA significantly improved fatigue, anxiety, and depression. SA did not improve fatigue or anxiety but improved depression.[[87](#cit/section_5.87)] The investigators did not directly compare EA with SA because the study was not powered to detect a difference, especially for secondary end points.

A post hoc analysis of a larger study showed that acupressure was associated with reduced anxiety, pain, and depressive symptoms when compared with usual care.[[86](#cit/section_5.86)]

#### Sleep

Several RCTs have studied the effect of acupuncture in improving depression and sleep quality in cancer patients. In 2011, one study (N = 80) reported that acupuncture significantly improved depression and sleep quality when compared with [fluoxetine](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45374&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov). Patients receiving acupuncture reported significantly greater reductions in scores on the Self-rating Depression Scale, Hamilton Depression Rating Scale, and Pittsburgh Sleep Quality Index.[[128](#cit/section_5.128)]

A 2019 RCT of cancer survivors reported that even though both [cognitive behavioral therapy](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=650559&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) for insomnia (CBT-I) and acupuncture produced a clinically significant reduction in insomnia severity, CBT-I is superior to acupuncture. Subgroup analyses showed that acupuncture was more effective than CBT-I in pain reduction, and CBT-I was more effective for improving insomnia in highly educated White males with no pain at baseline.[[129](#cit/section_5.129)] A secondary analysis of this trial evaluated the effect of acupuncture versus CBT-I on pain in cancer survivors with both insomnia and pain. Among the 70 cancer survivors, acupuncture (n = 35) reduced pain more significantly than CBT-I (n = 35) at week 8 (acupuncture, -1.4; 95% CI, -2.0 to -0.8; CBT-I, -0.4; 95% CI, -1.0 to 0.2). At 20-week follow-up, both groups showed significant pain reduction without intergroup difference.[[130](#cit/section_5.130)]

The effect of acupuncture on sleep has been studied as one of the secondary endpoints in an AIMSS study published in 2014.[[87](#cit/section_5.87)] Compared with wait-list controls, EA participants had a nonsignificant improvement in sleep disturbance (*P* = .058).

#### Other treatment-related side effects

In an RCT,[[135](#cit/section_5.135)] 76 patients with various types of cancer, including 38 with esophageal cancer, 24 with gastric cancer, and 14 with [lung cancer](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=445043&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), were randomly assigned to two groups (N = 38 per group). The treatment group received acupuncture in combination with radiation therapy or chemotherapy, and the control group was treated with radiation therapy or chemotherapy alone. The data showed that the patients in the acupuncture group gained significantly more body weight than patients in the control group (*P* < .001). In patients with lung cancer, the acupuncture group also showed greater improvement than the controls in the symptoms of cough, thoracodynia, hemoptysis, and fever; in patients with esophageal cancer, the acupuncture group showed greater improvement in the symptoms of chest pain, mucus vomiting, and difficulty in swallowing. In addition, the acupuncture group suffered fewer side effects (e.g., poor appetite, N/V, dizziness, or fatigue) from radiation therapy or chemotherapy than the control group. However, no statistical analysis was performed on these data.

A retrospective study involved patients at an oncology clinic who were offered acupuncture treatment for potential [palliation](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=467854&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) of symptoms.[[136](#cit/section_5.136)] Among 89 patients treated with acupuncture, 79 responded to a telephone questionnaire survey. The data indicated that the major reasons for [referral](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=640066&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) included pain (53%), xerostomia (32%), hot flashes (6%), and nausea/loss of appetite (6%). Sixty percent of the patients showed at least 30% improvement in their symptoms, and about one-third had no change in the severity of symptoms. Patients were not questioned about acupuncture-treatment expectations.

### Pediatric Population and Acupuncture

Few studies have examined the effects of acupuncture on [pediatric](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46245&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) patients with cancer. In a survey study of 80 patients, the acceptance rate for acupuncture was 82% in pediatric, [adolescent](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=776825&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), and young adult patients undergoing [hematopoietic stem cell](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=693540&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) [transplant](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46631&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov).[[142](#cit/section_5.142)] A 2018 RCT of 165 pediatric patients who received chemotherapy with high-emetogenic potential showed that acupressure bands at the PC6 point were safe but did not improve CINV when compared with placebo wrist bands.[[143](#cit/section_5.143)] An RCT of acupressure to reduce treatment-related symptoms in children with cancer and recipients of hematopoietic stem cell transplantation was initiated in October 2017. The study's [protocol](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44714&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) outline has been published.[[144](#cit/section_5.144)] No outcome data have been made available to date.

Adverse effects of acupuncture in children appear to be rare and limited to the same effects that are observed in adults.[[145](#cit/section_5.145)-[147](#cit/section_5.147)]. One study showed that there was no increased [incidence](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46145&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) of adverse events in children with [thrombocytopenia](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45365&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) or [neutropenia.](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45361&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) [[147](#cit/section_5.147)]

## Adverse Effects

Serious [adverse effects](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44922&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) of [acupuncture](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46724&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) are rare. Reported accidents and [infections](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45364&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) appear to be related to violations of [sterile](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46600&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) procedure, negligence of the [practitioner](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=463164&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), or both.[[1](#cit/section_6.1),[2](#cit/section_6.2)] A systematic review of [case reports](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44007&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) on the safety of acupuncture, involving 98 papers published in the English language from 22 countries during the period from 1965 to 1999, found only 202 incidents. The number of incidents appeared to decline as training standards and licensure requirements were enhanced. Among the 118 (60%) reported incidents involving infection, 94 (80%) involved [hepatitis](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46371&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), occurring mainly in the late 1970s and early 1980s. Very few hepatitis or other infections associated with acupuncture have been reported since 1988, when widespread use of disposable needles was introduced and national certification requirements for clean-needle techniques were developed and enforced as an acupuncture licensure requirement.[[3](#cit/section_6.3),[4](#cit/section_6.4)] Because [cancer](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45333&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) patients who are undergoing [chemotherapy](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45214&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) or [radiation therapy](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44971&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) are [immunocompromised](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45723&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), precautions must be taken and strict clean-needle techniques must be applied when acupuncture treatment is given.[[5](#cit/section_6.5)]

Minor adverse effects of acupuncture such as the following, have been reported:

- Pain at [needling](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=449723&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) sites.
- [Hematoma](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=449931&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov).
- Tiredness.
- Lightheadedness.
- Drowsiness.
- [Localized](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45754&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) skin irritation.

These minor adverse effects can be minimized by appropriate patient management, including local pressing and massage at the needling site after treatment.[[6](#cit/section_6.6),[7](#cit/section_6.7)]

## Summary of the Evidence for Acupuncture Treatment of Cancer-Related Symptoms

To assist readers in evaluating the results of [human studies](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44195&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) of integrative, alternative, and complementary therapies for [cancer](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45333&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), the strength of the evidence (i.e., the [levels of
evidence](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=446533&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov)) associated with each type of treatment is provided whenever
possible. To qualify for a level of evidence [analysis](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=390238&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), a study must:

- Be published in a [peer-reviewed scientific journal](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=537399&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov).
- Report on a [therapeutic](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=43985&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) [outcome](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=467853&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) or outcomes, such as [tumor](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46634&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) [response](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44085&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), improvement in survival, or measured improvement in [quality of life](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45417&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov).
- Describe [clinical](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44168&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) findings in sufficient detail that a meaningful evaluation can be made.

Separate levels of evidence scores are assigned to qualifying human studies on the basis of statistical strength of the study design and [scientific](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44724&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) strength of the treatment outcomes (i.e., [endpoints](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=346519&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov)) measured. The resulting two scores are then combined to produce an overall score. For an explanation of the scores and additional information about levels of evidence analysis, see the [Levels of Evidence for Human Studies of Integrative, Alternative, and Complementary Therapies](/publications/pdq/levels-evidence/cam).

It is noteworthy that almost all reported [clinical studies](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44195&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) on the effects of [acupuncture](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46724&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) on cancer or cancer [therapy](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44737&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov)–related [symptoms](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45022&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) focus on [symptom management](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=269453&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) rather than the disease itself. Investigations into the effects of acupuncture on [chemotherapy](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45214&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov)-induced [nausea](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=390302&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) and [vomiting](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=390324&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), many of which were [randomized](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45858&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) and well-controlled, produced the most convincing findings. A number of randomized [controlled trials](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44014&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) have reported on the effect of acupuncture in alleviating other cancer treatment-associated side effects, with many showing promising evidence supporting the use of acupuncture. Additional phase III clinical trials are ongoing.
