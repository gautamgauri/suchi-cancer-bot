# GATA2 Deficiency Syndrome (PDQ®) - NCI

# GATA2 Deficiency Syndrome (PDQ®)–Health Professional Version

## Introduction to GATA2 Deficiency Syndrome

Germline loss-of-function variants in the master hematopoietic [transcription](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=390290&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) factor, *GATA2*, can cause cellular deficiencies that have a high propensity to develop into myeloid malignancies. Initial reports identified GATA2 deficiency in cohorts of patients with the following conditions:

- [Familial](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=460148&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) myelodysplastic syndrome (MDS)/acute myeloid leukemia (AML).[[1](#cit/section_1.1)]
- [Autosomal dominant](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=793860&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) and sporadic monocytopenias and mycobacterial infection (MonoMAC) syndrome.[[2](#cit/section_1.2)]
- **D**endritic **c**ell, **m**onocyte, and B and natural killer (NK) **l**ymphoid (DCML) deficiency.[[3](#cit/section_1.3)]
- Lymphedema and anogenital dysplasia (WILD) syndrome.[[4](#cit/section_1.4)]
- Primary lymphedema associated with a predisposition to AML (Emberger syndrome).[[5](#cit/section_1.5)]
- Unexplained chronic neutropenia.[[6](#cit/section_1.6)]
- Primary childhood MDS.[[7](#cit/section_1.7)]

Following these initial reports, it became well accepted that all of these clinical phenotypes represent the broad spectrum of GATA2 deficiency, a single genetic disease.

## Genetics and Molecular Biology of GATA2 Deficiency Syndrome

GATA2 deficiency is caused by [de novo](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=783882&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) or inherited, heterozygous, [germline](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=460154&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) [pathogenic variants](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=783960&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) in the *GATA2* [gene](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45693&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic), which result in a loss of GATA2 expression or abnormal GATA2 transcription factor function.[[1](#cit/section_2.1)] Most patients with GATA2 deficiency carry null *GATA2* variants (i.e., [splice-site variants](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=783968&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic), [nonsense variants](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=783965&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic), [frameshift variants](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=783963&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic), whole-gene [deletions](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=460141&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic), or synonymous variants that affect [RNA splicing](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=460220&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic)) or *GATA2* [missense variants](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=783964&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) that affect the zinc finger 2 [domain](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=460145&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic). Approximately 10% of patients have noncoding substitutions in the EBOX-GATA-ETS enhancer element in [intron](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=660737&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) 4 of *GATA2*, or occasionally, tandem duplications of the entire *GATA2* [locus](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=460162&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic).[[1](#cit/section_2.1),[2](#cit/section_2.2)]

[Variable expressivity](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=339331&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) is common in GATA2 deficiency. Some patients present early in life with one of the following: 1) cytopenias and bone marrow failure that progress to myelodysplastic syndrome (MDS)/acute myeloid leukemia (AML), or 2) severe immunodeficiency with recurrent bacterial, viral, and fungal infections.[[3](#cit/section_2.3)-[9](#cit/section_2.9)] Other patients with GATA2 deficiency develop MDS with excess blasts without preexisting clinical features.[[10](#cit/section_2.10)-[12](#cit/section_2.12)] Immunodeficiency is common in patients with GATA2 deficiency and manifests with mycobacterial infections, human papillomavirus (HPV) infections (i.e., generalized warts, intraepithelial neoplasia), Epstein-Barr virus (EBV)–related disease, herpes virus–related disease, and fungal infections. Some patients may also develop pulmonary disease (anti-granulocyte–macrophage colony stimulating factor [GM-CSF] antibody-negative pulmonary alveolar proteinosis), thrombosis, and autoimmune features like autoimmune cytopenias, hepatitis, colitis, panniculitis, and arthritis. Immunological laboratory findings can include deficiencies of the following: dendritic cells, monocytes, transitional B cells, and natural killer cells. Patients with GATA2 deficiency can also have inverted CD4:CD8 ratios and hypogammaglobulinemia. Loss of B cells and their precursors was shown to be the most common feature in GATA2-deficient patients with MDS.[[12](#cit/section_2.12)] Although not all patients with GATA2 deficiency present with immunological changes, a history of immunodeficiency in a patient with MDS/AML may be a strong indicator that a germline *GATA2* pathogenic variant is present.[[3](#cit/section_2.3),[8](#cit/section_2.8),[13](#cit/section_2.13)]

## Clinical Phenotypes in GATA2 Deficiency Syndrome

Approximately half of reported patients with *GATA2* [pathogenic variants](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=783960&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) have constitutional features of GATA2 deficiency. Constitutional features of GATA2 deficiency include the following:[[1](#cit/section_3.1),[2](#cit/section_3.2)]

- [Congenital](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=460137&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) sensorineural deafness.
- Lymphedema.
- Hydrocele.
- Urogenital malformations.
- Attention deficit hyperactivity disorder (ADHD) spectrum disorders.

GATA2 deficiency prevalence is dependent upon several factors, including a patient's age and clinical manifestations. A myelodysplastic syndrome (MDS) cohort study estimated that [germline](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=460154&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) *GATA2* pathogenic variants were present in approximately 0.5% of adults and 7% of children.[[1](#cit/section_3.1)] Commonly acquired genetic lesions in GATA2 deficiency-related MDS include the following:[[3](#cit/section_3.3)-[8](#cit/section_3.8)]

- Abnormal karyotypes (monosomy 7, der(1;7) [translocation](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=470251&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic), and [trisomy](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=460223&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) 8).
- Somatic variants, particularly in the *ASXL1*, *SETBP1*, *STAG2*, and *RUNX1* [genes](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45693&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic).

In children with GATA2-related MDS, monosomy 7 has been reported in up to 70% to 80% of cases. However, in adults, the frequencies of both trisomy 8 and monosomy 7 were approximately 20% to 40%.[[9](#cit/section_3.9)] It is recommended that monosomy 7 in a young patient trigger *GATA2* germline testing, since up to 72% of adolescents with an MDS diagnosis and monosomy 7 carry germline *GATA2* pathogenic variants.[[1](#cit/section_3.1)] The der (1;7) [chromosomal](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46470&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) abnormality is a [cytogenetic](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=270737&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) lesion that is also enriched in patients with GATA2 deficiency.[[10](#cit/section_3.10)]

The lifetime risk of developing myeloid neoplasms (MDS, myeloproliferative neoplasms, acute myeloid leukemia [AML]) is very high in individuals with GATA2 deficiency. The median age of myeloid neoplasm diagnosis was estimated to be 12 years in pediatric cohorts [[1](#cit/section_3.1)] and 35 years in adult cohorts.[[5](#cit/section_3.5)] Consolidated data from several studies suggest that the average age of myeloid neoplasm diagnosis was 20 years,[[9](#cit/section_3.9)] and the median age of diagnosis is 17 years (range, 0–78 y).[[11](#cit/section_3.11)] Infants and young children are typically not affected with myeloid malignancy or clinically relevant immunodeficiencies. However, these symptoms can manifest as early as age 4 to 5 years.[[1](#cit/section_3.1)] The lifetime [penetrance](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=339344&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) of GATA2 deficiency is high (estimated as >80%), and thus far, only a small proportion of *GATA2* carriers reported not having symptoms.

[Genotype](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=660739&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic)/[phenotype](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=460203&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) correlative studies found that lymphedema is associated with *GATA2* null pathogenic variants.[[11](#cit/section_3.11)] However, correlations between *GATA2* variant type and hematopoietic malignancy development have not yet been established. Additional research is required to investigate whether different types of *GATA2* variants have differing penetrance rates.

## Management and Prognosis for GATA2 Deficiency Syndrome

Consensus guidelines on the management of GATA2 deficiency do not yet exist, and hence, [surveillance](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=496506&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) strategies are individually tailored for each patient. Most patients are followed by hematologists, immunologists, or transplant physicians. General GATA2 deficiency management recommendations include the following: periodic analysis of peripheral blood counts and immune status, yearly bone marrow evaluation with [cytogenetics](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=270737&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) and somatic variant testing, and [screening](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46171&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) for human papillomavirus (HPV)–related cancers.[[1](#cit/section_4.1),[2](#cit/section_4.2)] Patients with severe cellular deficiencies may require antimicrobial prophylaxis.[[3](#cit/section_4.3)] Due to the risk of clonal evolution, treatment with granulocyte colony-stimulating factor (G-CSF) is generally avoided in neutropenic patients with GATA2 deficiency.

It is widely accepted that timely hematopoietic stem cell transplantation (HSCT) is the only curative approach for symptomatic GATA2-deficient patients. HSCT outcomes are influenced by several patient factors, including myelodysplastic syndrome (MDS) subtype and the presence of a preexisting immunodeficiency. Overall survival (OS) rates for patients who underwent HSCT for various indications were reported as follows: 54% for MDS/acute myeloid leukemia (AML) or immunodeficiency,[[4](#cit/section_4.4)] 66% in children with MDS and monosomy 7,[[5](#cit/section_4.5)] 88% in children with refractory cytopenia of childhood (RCC) and normal karyotypes,[[6](#cit/section_4.6)] and 86% in young adults with immunodeficiency.[[7](#cit/section_4.7)] Difficulty in predicting the disease’s course complicates decision making regarding the optimal timing of HSCT. In 2021, researchers examined HSCT outcomes in 65 patients with GATA2-related MDS.[[8](#cit/section_4.8)] Five years after HSCT, the probability of OS and disease-free survival (DFS) was 75% and 70%, respectively. Nonrelapse mortality and relapse equally contributed to treatment failure. There was no evidence of increased graft-versus-host-disease incidence, excessive rates of infections, or organ toxicities. Advanced MDS and monosomy 7 were associated with worse outcomes. Patients who had RCC with normal karyotypes had better outcomes (DFS rate, 90%) when compared with those who had RCC and monosomy 7 (DFS rate, 67%).

HSCT is indicated in GATA2-deficient patients with the following: recurrent infections, transfusion dependency, or clonal evolution into a myeloid malignancy.[[9](#cit/section_4.9)-[11](#cit/section_4.11)] Several cases of donor-derived MDS/AML have been reported after HSCT in families with GATA2 deficiency.[[12](#cit/section_4.12),[13](#cit/section_4.13)] This emphasizes the need for [genetic counseling](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44961&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic), assessment for the presence of GATA2-specific clinical features, and genetic testing in potential [familial](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=460148&version=healthprofessional&language=English&dictionary=genetic) donors.
