# Pruritus - NCI

# Pruritus (PDQ®)–Patient Version

## General Information About Pruritus

[Go to Health Professional Version](/about-cancer/treatment/side-effects/skin-nail-changes/pruritus-hp-pdq)

### Key Points

- Certain conditions, cancers, and blood disorders may cause pruritus.

[Pruritus](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=446534&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) is an itchy feeling that makes you want to scratch your skin. It may occur without a rash or skin [lesions](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46324&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov). Pruritus sometimes feels like pain because the signals for itching and pain travel along the same [nerve](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=373935&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) pathways. Scratching may cause breaks in the skin, bleeding, and [infection](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45364&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov). If your skin feels itchy, let your doctor know so it can be treated and relieved.

The way pruritus feels and how long it lasts is not the same in everyone.

The skin is the largest [organ](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=257523&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) of the body. The most important job of skin is to protect against heat, sunlight, injury, and infection. The skin is also important to self-image and your ability to touch and be touched.

### Certain conditions, cancers, and blood disorders may cause pruritus.

[Pruritus](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=446534&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) is a [symptom](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45022&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) of a certain [condition](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=651193&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), [blood](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=270735&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) [disorder](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=407758&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), or a disease. These include:

- [Cancer](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45333&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) and conditions related to cancer.
- [Liver](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46312&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), [kidney](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46325&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), or [thyroid](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46036&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) disorders.
- [Diabetes mellitus](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44129&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov).
- [HIV](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44985&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) or [parasite](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44310&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) [infection](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45364&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov).
- Dry skin.
- [Drug](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=348921&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) reactions.
- Conditions related to [stress](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=450122&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), [anxiety](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=430405&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), or [depression](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=430479&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov).

The cause of pruritus is not always known.

## Causes of Pruritus in Cancer Patients

### Key Points

- Certain cancer treatments may cause pruritus.
- Drugs may be used for supportive care.

### Certain cancer treatments may cause pruritus.

Cancer treatments that may cause pruritus include [chemotherapy](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45214&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), [radiation therapy](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44971&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), and [immunotherapy](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45729&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) (biologic therapy).

- When chemotherapy causes pruritus, it may be a [sign](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=750109&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) that you are sensitive to the drugs being used.
- Radiation therapy can kill skin [cells](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46476&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) and cause dryness, burning, and itching as the skin peels off.
- Drugs used in immunotherapy may also cause dryness and itching.

Skin can become thin and dry because many of these [therapies](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44737&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) make your skin less able to make new cells and heal. Long-term dry skin may occur when hair and sweat [gland](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46386&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) function does not return to normal right after cancer treatment.

### Drugs may be used for supportive care.

Some of the drugs used to prevent or treat cancer [symptoms](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45022&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) may cause pruritus, including the following:

- Pain [medicine](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=482419&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) such as [opioids](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45054&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov).
- Drugs for [nausea](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=390302&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) and [vomiting](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=390324&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov).
- [Hormones](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45713&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) such as [estrogens](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46076&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), [testosterone](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45581&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), or [progestins](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=304743&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov).

## Assessment of Pruritus

### Key Points

- Finding the cause of the itching is the first step in relieving pruritus.
- A physical exam, blood tests, and a chest x-ray are done to assess pruritus.

### Finding the cause of the itching is the first step in relieving pruritus.

Since [pruritus](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=446534&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) is a [symptom](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45022&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) of a disease or [condition](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=651193&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), finding and treating the cause is the first step in bringing you relief.

### A physical exam, blood tests, and a chest x-ray are done to assess pruritus.

The following tests and procedures may be done to find the problem that is causing the itching:

- **[Physical exam](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=270871&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) and [history](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=689078&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov)**: An exam of the body to check general [signs](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=750109&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) of health, including checking for signs of disease, such as lumps or anything else that seems unusual. The doctor will check your skin for the following:

- Signs of [infection](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45364&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov).
- Signs of a [drug](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=348921&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) reaction.
- Redness, dryness, scratches, or [lesions](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46324&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov).
- [Abnormal](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44636&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) color, texture, or temperature.

A history of your health habits, past illnesses, and treatments will also be taken. You may be asked about the following:

- When the pruritus started, how long it lasts, how bad it is, and what part of your body is itchy.
- How it affects your [daily activities](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=430401&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) and sleep.
- What makes the itching better or worse.
- Whether other family members or pets are affected.
- Whether you have had pruritus before.
- Current [cancer](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45333&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) treatment or past history of cancer.
- Other diseases you have now or had in the past and their treatment.
- Pain [medicines](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=482419&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), [antibiotics](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45967&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), or other drugs you are taking, including illegal drugs.
- Whether your [diet](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44660&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) is healthy and you drink enough [fluids](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44669&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov).
- Social history (hobbies, job, sexual history, and travel).
- How you care for your skin.
- Your emotional health.
- **[Blood chemistry studies](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=304685&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov)**: A procedure in which a [blood](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=270735&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) sample is checked to measure the amounts of certain substances released into the blood by [organs](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=257523&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) and [tissues](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46683&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) in the body. An unusual (higher or lower than normal) amount of a substance can be a sign of disease. These blood tests include:

- [Kidney function tests](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=482348&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov).
- [Liver function tests](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=390293&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov).
- [Lactate dehydrogenase](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=354462&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) test.
- [Thyroid](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46036&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) function tests.
- **[Complete blood count (CBC) with differential](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=729979&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov)**: A procedure in which a sample of blood is drawn and checked for the following:

- The number of [red blood cells](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46124&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) and [platelets](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45840&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov).
- The number and type of [white blood cells](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45993&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov).
- The amount of [hemoglobin](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45108&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) (the [protein](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46092&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) that carries [oxygen](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=538149&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov)) in the red blood cells.
- The portion of the blood sample made up of red blood cells.
- **[Sedimentation rate](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=354461&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov)**: A procedure in which a sample of blood is drawn and checked for the rate at which the red blood cells settle to the bottom of the test tube. The sedimentation rate is a measure of how much [inflammation](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44042&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) is in the body. A higher than normal sedimentation rate may be a sign of [lymphoma](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45368&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) or another condition. Also called erythrocyte sedimentation rate, sed rate, or ESR.
- **[Chest x-ray](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=304687&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov)**: An [x-ray](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45944&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) of the organs and bones inside the chest. An x-ray is a type of energy beam that can go through the body and onto film, making a picture of areas inside the body.

Depending on the results, further tests, such as a skin [biopsy](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45164&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), may be done to [diagnose](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46450&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) the problem and decide on treatment.

## Treatment of Pruritus

### Key Points

- Treatment of pruritus in cancer patients involves learning what the triggers are and taking steps to avoid them.
- Different types of treatment are used to help treat pruritus.
- Self-care
- Over-the-counter treatments
- Cornstarch and talc
- Creams and lotions
- Prescription drugs applied to the skin
- Systemic therapies
- Other drug therapies
- Comfort measures

### Treatment of pruritus in cancer patients involves learning what the triggers are and taking steps to avoid them.

It is important for you and for [caregivers](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=648947&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) to know what [triggers](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=454808&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) itching, such as dry skin or hot baths, so you can take steps to prevent it. You may need more than one type of treatment to relieve or prevent [pruritus](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=446534&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), protect your skin, and keep you comfortable.

Good [nutrition](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44698&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) is very important for healthy skin. A good [diet](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44660&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) includes a balance of [proteins](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46092&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), [carbohydrates](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=285960&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), fats, [vitamins](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44744&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), [minerals](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45787&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), and [fluids](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44669&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov). Eating a balanced diet and drinking plenty of fluids helps your skin stay healthy. It is best to drink at least 3 [liters](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=655098&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) (about 100 [ounces](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=330174&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov)) of fluid each day, but this may not be possible for everyone.

Washing the skin every day or every two days is important to help remove dirt and keep the skin healthy.

### Different types of treatment are used to help treat pruritus.

#### Self-care

Self-care includes avoiding pruritus triggers and taking good care of your skin.

*Pruritus triggers include:*

- [Dehydration](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44659&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) caused by [fever](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=450108&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), [diarrhea](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=306496&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), [nausea](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=390302&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) and [vomiting](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=390324&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), or low fluid intake.
- Hot baths or bathing more than once a day, or for longer than 30 minutes.
- Bubble baths or soaps with detergents.
- Reusable scrubbing sponges for the face or loofahs for the body.
- Scents, fragrances, and perfumes.
- Adding oil at the beginning of the bath.
- Dry indoor air.
- Laundry detergent with scents, dyes, or preservatives.
- Fabric softener sheets.
- Tight clothes or clothes made of wool, synthetics, or other
harsh/scratchy fabric.
- Underarm deodorants or antiperspirants.
- Skin care or cosmetics with scents, dyes, or preservatives.
- Emotional [stress](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=450122&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov).

*Ways to help lessen itching include:*

- Using unscented, soothing creams or [ointments](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=508685&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov).
- Bathing in slightly warm water no more than 30 minutes daily or every other day.
- Using mild skin cleansers (non-soap) or soaps made for sensitive skin (such as Cetaphil cleanser, Dove for Sensitive Skin, Oilatum, Basis).
- Adding oil and soap at the end of a bath or adding a colloidal oatmeal treatment
early to the bath.
- Using soap only for dirty areas; otherwise water is good enough.
- Gently washing, if needed, with a clean, fresh, soft cotton washcloth.
- Rinsing all soap or other residue from bathing with fresh, slightly warm water.
- Drying off by patting skin instead of rubbing.
- Keeping home air cool and humid (including use of a [humidifier](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46362&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov)).
- Washing sheets, clothes, and underwear in mild soap or baby soap that contains no scents, dyes, or preservatives (such as Dreft, All Free Clear, Tide Free and Gentle). Adding vinegar (one teaspoon per quart of water) to rinse water removes traces of detergent.
- Using liquid fabric softener that gets rinsed out in the wash (such as All Free Clear Fabric Softener) or avoiding fabric softener altogether.
- Using blankets that are soft, such as cotton flannel.
- Wearing loose-fitting clothes and clothes made of cotton or other soft
fabrics.
- Using [distraction](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44663&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), [music therapy](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=640061&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), relaxation, or positive [imagery](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45719&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov).

#### Over-the-counter treatments

Some [over-the-counter](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44696&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) treatments ([medicines](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=482419&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) that can be bought without a [prescription](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44711&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov)) help prevent or relieve pruritus. However, you should read labels carefully to look for ingredients that may trigger skin reactions, including [alcohol](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=463134&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), [topical](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45927&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) [antibiotics](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45967&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), and topical [anesthetics](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46051&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov).

##### Cornstarch and talc

Cornstarch can help prevent itching of dry skin caused by [radiation therapy](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44971&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) but should not be used where skin is moist. When cornstarch becomes moist, [fungus](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44571&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) may grow. Avoid using it on areas close to [mucous membranes](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=257212&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), such as the [vagina](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46646&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) or [rectum](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46555&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), in skin folds, and on areas that have hair or sweat [glands](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46386&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov).

Some powders and antiperspirants, such as those that contain [talc](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=470254&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) and [aluminum](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=639941&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), cause skin irritation during radiation therapy and should be avoided when you're receiving radiation treatment.

For itching not related to radiation therapy, talc-based treatments may be better than cornstarch-based treatments, especially where two skin surfaces touch or rub together (such as the underarm or between fingers or toes).

##### Creams and lotions

If pruritus is related to dry skin, [emollient](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=781893&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) creams or lotions may be used. Emollients help soothe and soften the skin and increase moisture levels in the skin. It is important to know the ingredients in these creams and lotions because some
may cause skin reactions. Such ingredients include:

- [Petrolatum](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=476581&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), which is not well absorbed in skin treated with radiation therapy and may build up too much or be hard to remove.
- [Lanolin](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=486452&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), which may cause [allergic reactions](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=443292&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) in some people.
- [Mineral oil](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=486450&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), which may be combined with
petrolatum and lanolin in creams and lotions and may be an ingredient in bath oils.

Other ingredients added to emollients, such as
thickeners, preservatives, fragrances, and colorings, may also cause
allergic skin reactions.

Emollient creams
or lotions are applied at least two or three times a day and after
bathing. Gels with a local anesthetic (0.5%–5% [lidocaine](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=306506&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov)) can be used on some small areas as often as every 2 hours if you aren't sensitive to alcohol ingredients.

To soothe or cool areas of severe pruritus, over-the-counter products containing [menthol](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=640055&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), [camphor](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44001&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov), pramoxine, or [capsaicin](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45377&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) can be used. These products soothe, cool, and decrease the urge to scratch. Capsaicin-based [therapies](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44737&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) may work best in pruritus related to [nerve](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=373935&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) signals.

#### Prescription drugs applied to the skin

Your doctor may prescribe topical [steroids](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46601&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) (steroids applied to the skin) to reduce itching, but they cause thinning of the skin and make it more sensitive. They should be used only for pruritus related to [inflammation](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44042&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov). Topical steroids should not be used on skin being treated with radiation therapy, but may be used to relieve [inflamed](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44042&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) skin after radiation treatment ends.

For xerosis ([abnormally](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44636&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) dry skin) or keratoderma (a horn-like skin [condition](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=651193&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov)), moisturizer creams may be used to seal in moisture and peel off scaly layers of skin. Humectants with ingredients like salicylic acid, ammonium lactate, or [urea](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=471785&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) may improve skin smoothness but can cause stinging if applied to broken skin.

#### Systemic therapies

[Systemic therapies](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45922&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) travel through the bloodstream and reach and affect [cells](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46476&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) all over the body. They may help treat the condition causing your pruritus or help control your [symptoms](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45022&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov).

Your doctor may prescribe an [antibiotic](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45967&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) if your pruritus is caused by an [infection](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45364&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov). You may also be given an [oral](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44068&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) [antihistamine](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=468794&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) to relieve itching. A larger [dose](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44664&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) may sometimes be used at bedtime to help you sleep.

#### Other drug therapies

If other [drug](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=348921&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) treatments do not work to control pruritus, [sedatives](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=450118&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) and [antidepressants](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44105&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) are sometimes used.

[Aspirin](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45176&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) may relieve pruritus in some patients with [polycythemia vera](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=426418&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) but may increase pruritus in others. [Cimetidine](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=45184&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) alone or combined with aspirin may help control pruritus in patients with [Hodgkin lymphoma](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=270800&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) and polycythemia vera.

#### Comfort measures

Other steps may be taken to help you keep from scratching and stop the itch-scratch-itch cycle. These may include:

- Applying emollients to help prevent skin breakdown.
- A cool washcloth or ice held over the itchy area.
- Firm pressure on the itchy area, on the same area on the opposite side of the body, and at [acupressure](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46723&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) points.
- Rubbing or vibration on the itchy area.
- [Transcutaneous electrical nerve stimulation](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=44740&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov) (TENS) or [acupuncture](/Common/PopUps/popDefinition.aspx?id=46724&version=patient&language=English&dictionary=Cancer.gov).

### Clinical Trial Information

A clinical trial is a study to answer a scientific question, such as whether one treatment is better than another. Trials are based on past studies and what has been learned in the laboratory. Each trial answers certain scientific questions in order to find new and better ways to help cancer patients. During treatment clinical trials, information is collected about the effects of a new treatment and how well it works. If a clinical trial shows that a new treatment is better than one currently being used, the new treatment may become "standard." Patients may want to think about taking part in a clinical trial. Some clinical trials are open only to patients who have not started treatment.

Clinical trials can be found online at [NCI's website](https://www.cancer.gov/research/participate/clinical-trials "https://www.cancer.gov/research/participate/clinical-trials").
